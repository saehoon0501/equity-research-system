# L6 — Multi-Horizon Disposition: Swing Trade vs Long-Term Investment vs Both

Decision rules per name × per timeframe. How does a discretionary PM decide whether a given equity is best expressed as a weeks-to-months swing, a multi-year compounding hold, or a hybrid (core + tactical sleeve)? What characteristics empirically and philosophically push a name into which bucket? What changes a disposition once held?

## Section B — Distilled patterns

1. **Druckenmiller's default lens is ~18 months to 3 years** ("Most trades I put on, I think in terms of 18 months to three years" — Hard Lessons / Morgan Stanley 2026). This is the MEDIUM horizon — neither pure swing (days/weeks) nor multi-decade compounder. Names below this horizon are tactical; names above need a different mental model. (Source: [Hard Lessons transcript])

2. **Conviction is multi-layered and time-dependent.** The pound short went from $1.5B (one third of fund, swing-style) to $10B+ (multiple of fund, all-in) only when a SPECIFIC institutional catalyst arrived (Schlesinger's 9/15/92 remarks). Position size scales with thesis maturity, not initial idea quality. Same name → different disposition at different evidence stages. (Source: [Layers of Conviction])

3. **Volatility regime + price sensitivity to narrative determines swing vs long-term.** High-vol, narrative-reflexive names (e.g., commodity equities, biotech binary outcomes, AI thematic plays) are inherently swing candidates because the price is driven by the narrative cycle, not steady cash compounding. Low-vol structural compounders (Costco, Mastercard archetype) reward long-horizon hold; daily vol is noise. (Sources: [Reflexivity bubble dynamics — Novel Investor]; [Lynch six categories — Sven Carlin])

4. **Lynch's category map is the most operational classifier.** Fast growers (20-25%+) = swing/medium; stalwarts (10-12%) = long-term core but trim-and-add as price disconnects from fundamentals; cyclicals = swing-only (timed against cycle); turnarounds = swing/medium with binary catalyst; slow growers = income only. The category determines disposition before any chart work. (Source: [Lynch six categories — Sven Carlin])

5. **Buffett's "favorite holding period is forever" is conditional, not absolute.** From the 1990 letter: forever applies to "outstanding businesses with outstanding managements." If management or moat deteriorate, the same Buffett who held Coca-Cola for decades has exited (e.g., airlines 2020). The disposition is conditional on the business, not the buy decision. (Source: [Berkshire 1990 letter])

6. **Munger's tax math is the structural reason long-term wins for true compounders.** Holding 30 years at 15% with single terminal 35% tax = 13.3% net CAGR; paying 35% annually at the same gross rate = 9.75% net. A 3.5% per-year drag swamps any plausible alpha from tactical rotation IF the underlying is genuinely compounding. This makes the swing/long-term decision an after-tax economics problem, not just a strategy preference. (Source: [Munger sit-on-hands — Picture Perfect Portfolios])

7. **Soros's reflexivity implies that any thesis tied to a market-driven feedback loop is by definition a swing trade.** Reflexive setups (bubbles, momentum-driven re-ratings) have endogenous self-termination. Soros stayed in large positions "only days at a time" precisely because reflexive moves end without warning. Names whose price drives their fundamentals (capital-raising at high prices, customer acquisition fed by stock-as-currency M&A) cannot be long-term holds. (Source: [Alchemy of Finance — DayTrading.com])

8. **Druckenmiller's scale-in protocol: one-third on thesis, two-thirds on price confirmation.** "I'll go in with a third of a position and wait for price confirmation. When I get the technical signal, I go." This converts thesis risk into evidence-weighted exposure: the same name is a small swing trade until the market confirms, then a full-conviction position. (Source: [Real Vision notes — Macro Ops])

9. **Livermore's pyramid is the trader's analog of staged conviction.** Start with a 20% test, add 4-5 blocks each only after the previous shows open profit, never average down. Each addition must be EARNED. This is the explicit-rule version of Druckenmiller's discretionary scale-in. (Source: [Livermore pyramiding])

10. **Tudor Jones uses a TIME STOP, not just a price stop.** "If I think a market should break, and it doesn't, I will often get out even if I'm not losing money." Time stops are diagnostic for swing trades: if the catalyst window passes without the expected move, the thesis is wrong by silence. Long-term holds do not have a time stop — they have a thesis-break stop. This is the cleanest mechanical separator between the two dispositions. (Source: [PTJ 21 rules])

11. **Marks's three legitimate sell triggers: thesis deteriorates, superior opportunity, concentration too large.** Note what's NOT on the list: "the stock is up a lot." For a long-term hold, price appreciation alone is never a sell. For a swing trade, hitting a price target IS a legitimate exit because the thesis was about the move, not the asset. The sell-rule set tells you which disposition you actually had. (Source: [Marks "Selling Out"])

12. **Klarman: 10-15 positions is enough, and rotation is conditional on materially better opportunity.** This implicitly defines "long-term" as multi-year by default — you only sell when something better forces the swap. He's the limit case of low-turnover discretion: every position is long-term until the opportunity set changes. (Source: [Margin of Safety — 30 ideas])

13. **The disposition effect is empirically backwards from optimal.** Odean: investors are 60% more likely to sell winners than losers. Jegadeesh-Titman momentum: winners over 6-12 months tend to keep winning. Combining the two: the empirical retail behavior is to truncate winners (which had positive expected continuation) and ride losers (which had negative continuation). PMs must explicitly fight this — the "right" disposition for a winning swing is to RIDE it via trailing stop, not book the gain. (Sources: [Disposition Effect Wikipedia]; [Jegadeesh-Titman])

14. **Time-series momentum confirms 12 months as a natural swing/intermediate horizon.** Moskowitz/Ooi/Pedersen across 67 markets: past-12-month return predicts next-12-month return; partial reversal beyond. So if a name is being traded for trend, 12 months is the academically-supported holding window. Beyond that you're either (a) trading mean-reversion or (b) holding for compounding fundamentals — different disposition. (Source: [Time Series Momentum])

15. **Lo's adaptive markets hypothesis: the right horizon for the same name CHANGES with regime.** A name that is a long-term hold in a stable productivity-led bull market may become a swing trade during a regime shift (rates, inflation, dollar). The disposition is not a property of the company alone — it's the (company × regime) tuple. (Source: [AMH — Lo MIT PDF])

16. **The "core + tactical sleeve" framework is empirically grounded by value/momentum negative correlation.** Asness/Moskowitz/Pedersen showed value and momentum are negatively correlated within and across asset classes. Translation: holding the SAME high-quality name as a long-term value/quality core, while running momentum-driven tactical trims/adds, harvests two uncorrelated alphas on one underlying. This is the mechanical justification for "both" as a disposition. (Source: [Value and Momentum Everywhere])

17. **Tax-aware disposition: the 1-year mark matters mechanically, not philosophically.** Short-term capital gains are taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 37%); long-term at 0/15/20%. For a winning swing trade nearing the 1-year mark, the after-tax breakeven is whatever price decline would offset ~17 percentage points of tax differential. This pulls the optimal exit forward only if the position has materially deteriorated. (Source: [IRS Topic 409])

18. **Druckenmiller fights analysis paralysis: "knowing when to stop analysing" is the hardest skill.** Implication for disposition: deciding swing vs long-term is itself a decision under uncertainty, and refusing to commit is its own bias. A PM who can't classify the name is implicitly trading it as long-term by default and losing optionality. (Source: [Hard Lessons transcript])

19. **Position sizing rule across all horizons (Mauboussin/Kelly): f = edge/odds, capped well below full Kelly.** Swing trades typically have higher edge (concentrated catalysts) but shorter horizon → can carry larger position weight transiently. Long-term holds have smaller per-period edge but compound → smaller transient weight, larger time-weighted exposure. Same Kelly logic, different time integration. (Source: [Mauboussin Size Matters])

20. **The PnL-anecdote trap.** Druckenmiller's specific bets (the pound, the bonds short, NVDA early-2024) make for rich narrative but they're outcome-conditioned recountings. The framework-quality content is in the rules — sizing scales with conviction, scaling-in requires price confirmation, time-stop separates swing from hold, thesis-break is the only sell criterion. Skill builders should reference the rule, not the anecdote. (Editor's note across sources)

## Section C — Open questions / disagreements

### C1. Concentrated-forever school (Buffett/Munger/Klarman) vs tactical-rotation school (Druckenmiller/Soros/Tudor Jones)

- **Buffett/Munger position**: Once you've identified a true compounder, "lethargy bordering on sloth" wins because (a) tax-deferral math, (b) reinvestment-at-high-IRR is doing the work, (c) most rotation underperforms the original asset. Marks broadly aligns: don't sell on price, sell on thesis deterioration.
- **Druckenmiller/Soros position**: Regimes shift. Holding the SAME 20% position across a regime change is a mistake. "I tend to change my mind every three weeks." Reflexive setups are inherently temporary. Concentration is correct, but concentration on the CURRENT best idea, which rotates.
- **Where they actually disagree**: On whether the "true compounder" is identifiable ex ante. Buffett claims yes (Coca-Cola, See's, Apple late). Soros essentially claims no — every thesis is provisional. Druckenmiller is in between: some businesses warrant 3+ year horizons, but you must be willing to abandon them in days.
- **Practical reconciliation**: The disagreement collapses if disposition is REVISITED on a defined cadence (quarterly re-underwrite) rather than committed at entry.

### C2. Price-target exits vs trailing-stop exits

- **Price target school** (most swing trading literature, Lynch's "fast grower hits PE expansion target"): Pre-define the move you're trying to capture; exit at target.
- **Trailing-stop school** (Druckenmiller, Livermore, trend followers): Let winners run via a moving stop; never cap the upside.
- The disagreement is sharpest for the same setup (a name in a strong trend approaching nominal price target). Price-target exit will systematically truncate the right tail; trailing stop will systematically give back end-of-trend profits. Neither is dominant — depends on the alpha profile of the underlying.

### C3. Time stops — useful for swing only, or for long-term too?

- Tudor Jones: explicit time stop on swing trades.
- Buffett/Munger: explicitly NO time stop on long-term — time is your friend.
- Open question: does a long-term thesis that hasn't played out in (say) 5 years count as broken? Klarman/Marks framework would say yes, IF a better opportunity has emerged. Buffett would say no — measure against the business performance, not the stock.

### C4. Does narrative reflexivity make a name PERMANENTLY swing-only?

- Soros's reflexivity argument suggests names with self-reinforcing price/fundamentals loops (capital raises into stock, M&A in stock, customer acquisition fed by perceived dominance) cannot be long-term compounders.
- Counter-argument: many of today's "compounders" went through reflexive bubble phases (Amazon dot-com, Tesla 2020, NVDA 2024) but emerged with durable economics. The reflexivity ended; the business persisted.
- Unresolved: ex ante, can you tell which reflexive setup will leave a durable business behind?

### C5. The disposition effect — is fighting it always optimal?

- Odean and most behavioral finance: yes — investors are systematically wrong about which to sell.
- Rational-disposition counter (Cici, science direct paper): in some information-asymmetry models, the empirical disposition pattern is rational.
- The PM-relevant resolution: in the absence of new information, fighting the disposition effect is the right default. With new information that updates either thesis or relative ranking, the "winners" can legitimately become sells.

### Operating notes for skill builders

- **Default classifier**: Use Lynch's six categories first (fast grower / stalwart / cyclical / turnaround / asset play / slow grower). The category alone determines the natural horizon for ~70% of names.
- **Override conditions**: Reflexivity (Soros), regime shift (Druckenmiller/Lo), thesis break (Marks/Buffett) — any one forces re-classification.
- **Mechanical separators**: Time stop (PTJ) defines swing; thesis-break-only stop defines long-term; both-running-in-parallel defines a "both" disposition.
- **Sizing protocol across horizons**: One-third initial → confirm → pyramid (Druckenmiller/Livermore). Cap at portfolio policy max (Klarman 10-15 positions implies ~10% cap).
- **Disposition is revisited**, not committed at entry. Quarterly re-underwrite cadence is the empirical fix for the Buffett/Druckenmiller philosophical clash.
