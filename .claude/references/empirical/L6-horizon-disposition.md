# L6 — Multi-Horizon Disposition: Swing Trade vs Long-Term Investment vs Both

Decision rules per name × per timeframe. How does a discretionary PM decide whether a given equity is best expressed as a weeks-to-months swing, a multi-year compounding hold, or a hybrid (core + tactical sleeve)? What characteristics empirically and philosophically push a name into which bucket? What changes a disposition once held?

## Section B — Distilled patterns

1. **Druckenmiller's default lens is ~18 months to 3 years** ("Most trades I put on, I think in terms of 18 months to three years" — Hard Lessons / Morgan Stanley 2026). This is the MEDIUM horizon — neither pure swing (days/weeks) nor multi-decade compounder. Names below this horizon are tactical; names above need a different mental model. (Source: [Hard Lessons transcript])

2. **Conviction is multi-layered and time-dependent.** The pound short went from $1.5B (one third of fund, swing-style) to $10B+ (multiple of fund, all-in) only when a SPECIFIC institutional catalyst arrived (Schlesinger's 9/15/92 remarks). Position size scales with thesis maturity, not initial idea quality. Same name → different disposition at different evidence stages. (Source: [Layers of Conviction])

3. **Volatility regime + price sensitivity to narrative determines swing vs long-term.** High-vol, narrative-reflexive names (e.g., commodity equities, biotech binary outcomes, AI thematic plays) are inherently swing candidates because the price is driven by the narrative cycle, not steady cash compounding. Low-vol structural compounders (Costco, Mastercard archetype) reward long-horizon hold; daily vol is noise. (Sources: [Reflexivity bubble dynamics — Novel Investor]; [Lynch six categories — Sven Carlin])

4. **Lynch's category map is the most operational classifier.** Fast growers (20-25%+) = swing/medium; stalwarts (10-12%) = long-term core but trim-and-add as price disconnects from fundamentals; cyclicals = swing-only (timed against cycle); turnarounds = swing/medium with binary catalyst; slow growers = income only. The category determines disposition before any chart work. (Source: [Lynch six categories — Sven Carlin])

5. **Buffett's "favorite holding period is forever" is conditional, not absolute.** From the 1990 letter: forever applies to "outstanding businesses with outstanding managements." If management or moat deteriorate, the same Buffett who held Coca-Cola for decades has exited (e.g., airlines 2020). The disposition is conditional on the business, not the buy decision. (Source: [Berkshire 1990 letter])

6. **Munger's tax math is the structural reason long-term wins for true compounders.** Holding 30 years at 15% with single terminal 35% tax = 13.3% net CAGR; paying 35% annually at the same gross rate = 9.75% net. A 3.5% per-year drag swamps any plausible alpha from tactical rotation IF the underlying is genuinely compounding. This makes the swing/long-term decision an after-tax economics problem, not just a strategy preference. (Source: [Munger sit-on-hands — Picture Perfect Portfolios])

7. **Soros's reflexivity implies that any thesis tied to a market-driven feedback loop is by definition a swing trade.** Reflexive setups (bubbles, momentum-driven re-ratings) have endogenous self-termination. Soros stayed in large positions "only days at a time" precisely because reflexive moves end without warning. Names whose price drives their fundamentals (capital-raising at high prices, customer acquisition fed by stock-as-currency M&A) cannot be long-term holds. (Source: [Alchemy of Finance — DayTrading.com])

8. **Druckenmiller's scale-in protocol: one-third on thesis, two-thirds on price confirmation.** "I'll go in with a third of a position and wait for price confirmation. When I get the technical signal, I go." This converts thesis risk into evidence-weighted exposure: the same name is a small swing trade until the market confirms, then a full-conviction position. (Source: [Real Vision notes — Macro Ops])

9. **Livermore's pyramid is the trader's analog of staged conviction.** Start with a 20% test, add 4-5 blocks each only after the previous shows open profit, never average down. Each addition must be EARNED. This is the explicit-rule version of Druckenmiller's discretionary scale-in. (Source: [Livermore pyramiding])

10. **Tudor Jones uses a TIME STOP, not just a price stop.** "If I think a market should break, and it doesn't, I will often get out even if I'm not losing money." Time stops are diagnostic for swing trades: if the catalyst window passes without the expected move, the thesis is wrong by silence. Long-term holds do not have a time stop — they have a thesis-break stop. This is the cleanest mechanical separator between the two dispositions. (Source: [PTJ 21 rules])

11. **Marks's three legitimate sell triggers: thesis deteriorates, superior opportunity, concentration too large.** Note what's NOT on the list: "the stock is up a lot." For a long-term hold, price appreciation alone is never a sell. For a swing trade, hitting a price target IS a legitimate exit because the thesis was about the move, not the asset. The sell-rule set tells you which disposition you actually had. (Source: [Marks "Selling Out"])

12. **Klarman: 10-15 positions is enough, and rotation is conditional on materially better opportunity.** This implicitly defines "long-term" as multi-year by default — you only sell when something better forces the swap. He's the limit case of low-turnover discretion: every position is long-term until the opportunity set changes. (Source: [Margin of Safety — 30 ideas])

13. **The disposition effect is empirically backwards from optimal.** Odean: investors are 60% more likely to sell winners than losers. Jegadeesh-Titman momentum: winners over 6-12 months tend to keep winning. Combining the two: the empirical retail behavior is to truncate winners (which had positive expected continuation) and ride losers (which had negative continuation). PMs must explicitly fight this — the "right" disposition for a winning swing is to RIDE it via trailing stop, not book the gain. (Sources: [Disposition Effect Wikipedia]; [Jegadeesh-Titman])

14. **Time-series momentum confirms 12 months as a natural swing/intermediate horizon.** Moskowitz/Ooi/Pedersen across 67 markets: past-12-month return predicts next-12-month return; partial reversal beyond. So if a name is being traded for trend, 12 months is the academically-supported holding window. Beyond that you're either (a) trading mean-reversion or (b) holding for compounding fundamentals — different disposition. (Source: [Time Series Momentum])

15. **Lo's adaptive markets hypothesis: the right horizon for the same name CHANGES with regime.** A name that is a long-term hold in a stable productivity-led bull market may become a swing trade during a regime shift (rates, inflation, dollar). The disposition is not a property of the company alone — it's the (company × regime) tuple. (Source: [AMH — Lo MIT PDF])

16. **The "core + tactical sleeve" framework is empirically grounded by value/momentum negative correlation.** Asness/Moskowitz/Pedersen showed value and momentum are negatively correlated within and across asset classes. Translation: holding the SAME high-quality name as a long-term value/quality core, while running momentum-driven tactical trims/adds, harvests two uncorrelated alphas on one underlying. This is the mechanical justification for "both" as a disposition. (Source: [Value and Momentum Everywhere])

17. **Tax-aware disposition: the 1-year mark matters mechanically, not philosophically.** Short-term capital gains are taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 37%); long-term at 0/15/20%. For a winning swing trade nearing the 1-year mark, the after-tax breakeven is whatever price decline would offset ~17 percentage points of tax differential. This pulls the optimal exit forward only if the position has materially deteriorated. (Source: [IRS Topic 409])

18. **Druckenmiller fights analysis paralysis: "knowing when to stop analysing" is the hardest skill.** Implication for disposition: deciding swing vs long-term is itself a decision under uncertainty, and refusing to commit is its own bias. A PM who can't classify the name is implicitly trading it as long-term by default and losing optionality. (Source: [Hard Lessons transcript])

19. **Position sizing rule across all horizons (Mauboussin/Kelly): f = edge/odds, capped well below full Kelly.** Swing trades typically have higher edge (concentrated catalysts) but shorter horizon → can carry larger position weight transiently. Long-term holds have smaller per-period edge but compound → smaller transient weight, larger time-weighted exposure. Same Kelly logic, different time integration. (Source: [Mauboussin Size Matters])

20. **The PnL-anecdote trap.** Druckenmiller's specific bets (the pound, the bonds short, NVDA early-2024) make for rich narrative but they're outcome-conditioned recountings. The framework-quality content is in the rules — sizing scales with conviction, scaling-in requires price confirmation, time-stop separates swing from hold, thesis-break is the only sell criterion. Skill builders should reference the rule, not the anecdote. (Editor's note across sources)

## Section C — Open questions / disagreements

### C1. Concentrated-forever school (Buffett/Munger/Klarman) vs tactical-rotation school (Druckenmiller/Soros/Tudor Jones)

- **Buffett/Munger position**: Once you've identified a true compounder, "lethargy bordering on sloth" wins because (a) tax-deferral math, (b) reinvestment-at-high-IRR is doing the work, (c) most rotation underperforms the original asset. Marks broadly aligns: don't sell on price, sell on thesis deterioration.
- **Druckenmiller/Soros position**: Regimes shift. Holding the SAME 20% position across a regime change is a mistake. "I tend to change my mind every three weeks." Reflexive setups are inherently temporary. Concentration is correct, but concentration on the CURRENT best idea, which rotates.
- **Where they actually disagree**: On whether the "true compounder" is identifiable ex ante. Buffett claims yes (Coca-Cola, See's, Apple late). Soros essentially claims no — every thesis is provisional. Druckenmiller is in between: some businesses warrant 3+ year horizons, but you must be willing to abandon them in days.
- **Practical reconciliation**: The disagreement collapses if disposition is REVISITED on a defined cadence (quarterly re-underwrite) rather than committed at entry.

### C2. Price-target exits vs trailing-stop exits

- **Price target school** (most swing trading literature, Lynch's "fast grower hits PE expansion target"): Pre-define the move you're trying to capture; exit at target.
- **Trailing-stop school** (Druckenmiller, Livermore, trend followers): Let winners run via a moving stop; never cap the upside.
- The disagreement is sharpest for the same setup (a name in a strong trend approaching nominal price target). Price-target exit will systematically truncate the right tail; trailing stop will systematically give back end-of-trend profits. Neither is dominant — depends on the alpha profile of the underlying.

### C3. Time stops — useful for swing only, or for long-term too?

- Tudor Jones: explicit time stop on swing trades.
- Buffett/Munger: explicitly NO time stop on long-term — time is your friend.
- Open question: does a long-term thesis that hasn't played out in (say) 5 years count as broken? Klarman/Marks framework would say yes, IF a better opportunity has emerged. Buffett would say no — measure against the business performance, not the stock.

### C4. Does narrative reflexivity make a name PERMANENTLY swing-only?

- Soros's reflexivity argument suggests names with self-reinforcing price/fundamentals loops (capital raises into stock, M&A in stock, customer acquisition fed by perceived dominance) cannot be long-term compounders.
- Counter-argument: many of today's "compounders" went through reflexive bubble phases (Amazon dot-com, Tesla 2020, NVDA 2024) but emerged with durable economics. The reflexivity ended; the business persisted.
- Unresolved: ex ante, can you tell which reflexive setup will leave a durable business behind?

### C5. The disposition effect — is fighting it always optimal?

- Odean and most behavioral finance: yes — investors are systematically wrong about which to sell.
- Rational-disposition counter (Cici, science direct paper): in some information-asymmetry models, the empirical disposition pattern is rational.
- The PM-relevant resolution: in the absence of new information, fighting the disposition effect is the right default. With new information that updates either thesis or relative ranking, the "winners" can legitimately become sells.

### Operating notes for skill builders

- **Default classifier**: Use Lynch's six categories first (fast grower / stalwart / cyclical / turnaround / asset play / slow grower). The category alone determines the natural horizon for ~70% of names.
- **Override conditions**: Reflexivity (Soros), regime shift (Druckenmiller/Lo), thesis break (Marks/Buffett) — any one forces re-classification.
- **Mechanical separators**: Time stop (PTJ) defines swing; thesis-break-only stop defines long-term; both-running-in-parallel defines a "both" disposition.
- **Sizing protocol across horizons**: One-third initial → confirm → pyramid (Druckenmiller/Livermore). Cap at portfolio policy max (Klarman 10-15 positions implies ~10% cap).
- **Disposition is revisited**, not committed at entry. Quarterly re-underwrite cadence is the empirical fix for the Buffett/Druckenmiller philosophical clash.

## Section B (continued) — Additional patterns

21. **Princeton-Newport's empirical record IS the Kelly track record.** Thorp's hedge fund ran fractional-Kelly across non-correlated arbitrage bets and produced 15.8% annualized at 4.3% standard deviation over 230 months (vs S&P 10.1% at 17.3% over the same era), profitable in 227 of 230 months. The empirical case for Kelly is not academic — it's a real fund with audited returns. (Source: [Compounding My Interests — Thorp track record])

22. **Full Kelly is empirically too aggressive even when the edge is real.** Carta-Conversano simulation: full-Kelly max drawdown 48.4% on single equities vs 25.0% under half-Kelly; triple-Kelly led to near-certain wealth deterioration. Thorp himself wrote that half-Kelly captures "about 3/4 of the growth with far less volatility." Half-Kelly is the empirical default; quarter-Kelly is sometimes preferred for live discretionary books where the edge estimate has wider error bars. (Sources: [Carta-Conversano Frontiers]; [Thorp — Kelly and the Stock Market])

23. **Vince's optimal-f reframes sizing around drawdown rather than variance.** Mean-variance treats a 30% drawdown and a 30% upspike symmetrically; optimal-f treats drawdown as the binding constraint. For PMs serving allocators with a hard drawdown threshold (endowments, family offices, pod-shop platforms), Vince's framework is the operationally honest sizing model. (Source: [Vince — Leverage Space Trading Model])

24. **Pod-shop drawdown stops are mechanical, not discretionary.** Millennium: 5% drawdown from allocated capital → 50% capital cut; 7.5% → pod terminated. Citadel and Point72 use individually negotiated thresholds with similar mechanics. PM turnover at Millennium is 15-20% annually; only 55% of PMs reach the three-year mark. The disposition implication: at a pod, EVERY position is implicitly a swing, because a slow-bleeding long-term hold can trigger the stop before the thesis matures. (Sources: [Young and Calculated]; [Net Interest Peak Pod])

25. **Pod-shop sizing forces sector- and factor-neutral pairings.** Because the platform requires beta- and factor-neutrality, single-name long-term concentrated bets are structurally banned. The pod expresses every name as a paired swing (long X, short Y same sector). "Conviction long-only multi-year hold" is not a disposition the pod is allowed to have — it lives on the multi-strat firm's "center book" if at all. (Sources: [Wall Street Oasis multi-manager risk limits]; [Bawa pod structure])

26. **Multi-manager center books capture cross-pod conviction.** Citadel and Millennium both run a "center book" that replicates high-conviction trades aggregated from across pods. This is where the long-horizon dispositions actually live in a multi-strat — funded out of the platform's risk budget, not the individual pod's. (Source: [Net Interest — Peak Pod])

27. **Long-term capital gains differential is mechanically ~17pp at top ordinary bracket.** STCG taxed up to 37%; LTCG capped at 20% (+3.8% NIIT for high earners). For a position with a large unrealized gain approaching the 1-year mark, the after-tax breakeven is whatever price decline would offset ~17pp of tax differential. Selling 364 days into the holding period is a >$170K-per-million-of-gain mistake at the top bracket. (Sources: [IRS Topic 409]; [NerdWallet 2025-2026 brackets])

28. **State tax can double the disposition penalty for high-tax-state PMs.** California and New York treat capital gains as ordinary income at state level; California top combined rate is ~13.3% on top of federal. A California-resident PM faces effective combined ~37.1% on STCG and ~33.3% on LTCG (federal LTCG 20% + NIIT 3.8% + CA 9.3% on the same gain). The horizon-shifting incentive is amplified: STCG/LTCG differential isn't 17pp, it's closer to 4pp at state, but the absolute drag is materially worse. (Sources: [Russell Investments CA tax]; [Entrust state capital gains rates])

29. **Wash-sale-rule-aware tax-loss harvesting requires a "substantially-different-but-similar" proxy.** The legal standard (per ASKramer): a knowledgeable investor "would discern a sufficiently material economic difference between two positions." Practical: sell single-stock loss → buy sector ETF (SPDR XLK, XLE etc.) → wait 31 days → swap back. The disposition implication: tax-loss harvesting can be a SCHEDULED non-fundamental disposition action that doesn't break the long-term thesis. (Sources: [ASKramer Tax-Loss Harvesting Part II]; [Fidelity wash-sale guide])

30. **Wash-sale loss is deferred to cost basis, NOT lost — except for IRA repurchase.** Disallowed loss adds back to replacement security's basis and the holding period is tacked on. For an IRA repurchase of the loss-sold security, the loss is permanently lost. Operationally: never harvest losses in a taxable account by repurchasing the same name in an IRA within the 61-day window. (Source: [Wash sale Wikipedia])

31. **Buffett's KO is the textbook "right" hold-through case.** 1988 entry $592M, scaled to 400M shares / $1.3B by 1994. Through 2026: ~$11.7B in cumulative dividends + ~$28B market value on the original $1.3B basis. The annual dividend per original-cost share now exceeds 60% — i.e., Buffett earns ~60% per year on the 1988 invested dollar in DIVIDENDS ALONE. The compounding is unrecoverable if interrupted by a tactical exit. (Source: [24/7 Wall St KO retrospective])

32. **Buffett's IBM is the textbook "wrong" hold-through case.** 2011 entry at avg $170; 7-year hold; ~94.5% exit by 2018 at $140-150. Underperformed S&P by ~134pp over the holding window. The thesis broke (cloud transition) but Buffett held longer than thesis-break alone would warrant — the position became a default-to-LT-by-inertia name. Three labeled mistakes: past-achievement anchoring, buyback-cheerleading, drift from circle of competence. (Source: [Motley Fool — IBM 3 mistakes])

33. **Buffett's airlines 2016-2020 is the contested case — process vs outcome.** Bought 2016, exited Q1-Q2 2020 at ~$5B realized loss vs counterfactual hold-through. Outcome was clearly unfavorable; the process question is whether the COVID information set warranted exit. Resolution: under genuine regime-shift uncertainty (Druckenmiller / Lo), the exit is defensible even with hindsight loss; the process and the outcome can disagree. (Source: [Motley Fool — Airlines retrospective])

34. **Buffett's Apple 2016-2024 is the partial-right case with self-acknowledged trim error.** 2016 entry (Combs/Weschler-led, not Buffett); built to ~$36B by 2018; ~⅔ trimmed during 2023-24. Buffett later admitted "I sold it too soon," explicitly comparing to AmEx (~30 yr) and KO (~35 yr) holding-period benchmarks. Implication: even disciplined LT investors trim winners under tax-rationale or concentration-rationale and regret it. (Sources: [24/7 Wall St — sold too soon]; [Read Trung — Apple bet history])

35. **The Buffett four-case pattern: thesis durability dominates entry quality.** KO (durable moat + management): hold worked. IBM (broken moat, missed transition): hold failed because thesis-break wasn't acted on. Airlines (regime shock): exit was process-correct under uncertainty. Apple (durable so far, but Buffett's own size-up exit): partial loss of compounding. Disposition lesson: revisit the thesis quarterly, not the price. (Synthesized from [KO retrospective]; [IBM 3 mistakes]; [Airlines retrospective]; [Apple sold too soon])

36. **Pershing Square = "concentrated long + event overlay" archetype.** 8-12 core positions, 4.8-year average holding period, layered with episodic large hedges (2020 $27M-to-$2.6B credit hedge). The hedges are themselves multi-month event trades. Two horizons run on the same book: long compounder core, swing event overlay. (Source: [Quartr Pershing Square])

37. **Greenlight Einhorn = "long-short by name, not by pair."** Longs are 1s/2s on Einhorn's idea-quality scale; shorts are 9s/10s. Explicit rejection of 4-vs-7 pair-trade structure that loses idiosyncratic alpha. Macro book is layered separately to manage gross/net exposure. The textbook way to run multi-horizon discretion when the alpha is in security selection. (Source: [Colossus — Einhorn])

38. **Tepper Appaloosa = "credit-to-equity rotation across the cycle."** Distressed bonds and preferreds convertible into equity provide an inherent multi-horizon book: hold the credit through the workout (multi-year), convert to equity at the cure point, then trade the equity (1-3 years) with macro overlays. The asset rotates as the situation matures. (Sources: [Appaloosa Wikipedia]; [25iq — Tepper])

39. **Loeb Third Point = "activist campaigns + risk-arb tactical sleeve."** ~46 holdings (more diversified than Pershing Square); separation between months-to-years activist campaigns and weeks-to-months catalyst-oriented event positions. The disposition is explicit per-position based on situation type. (Sources: [Loeb Wikipedia]; [Valens — Loeb new strategy])

40. **Klarman Baupost = "cash-heavy + concentrated long" archetype, quantified.** Historical asset mix (1995-2001): cash 10-50%, distressed debt 10-40%, equities 20-60%. Top 10 positions ~75% of disclosed equity assets. Cash IS a position with explicit optionality value; concentration is reserved for true mispricings. The hybrid resolution to the Marks/Druckenmiller polarity: stay long-term within positions, but let cash level breathe with the opportunity set. (Source: [Future Blind — Baupost allocations])

41. **Myopic loss aversion (Benartzi-Thaler 1995) is the empirical foundation for why retail investors collapse all timeframes into "today's move."** When subjects evaluate frequently, they take less equity risk; the equity premium itself is consistent with ~12-month evaluation cadence. Implication: a PM who CAN choose evaluation cadence (quarterly re-underwrite, not daily P&L watching) reclaims the premium that retail loses. (Sources: [Benartzi-Thaler 1995 NBER]; [Thaler-Tversky-Kahneman-Schwartz experimental test])

42. **Time diversification is partially a fallacy — Samuelson was mostly right.** Variance of cumulative return INCREASES with horizon even though variance of annualized return falls. Under isoelastic utility, optimal equity allocation does NOT depend on horizon. The only economically defensible horizon-driven risk-up argument is human capital: younger workers with bond-like future earnings can carry more equity. Implication for PM disposition: "I can take more risk because I'm long-term" is mostly a behavioral rationalization. Risk should be sized by edge × adverse-outcome capacity, not by horizon. (Sources: [SOA Davis "Myth of Time Diversification"]; [Norstad/Dan Luu]; [Rekenthaler])

43. **Cole's vol-regime point: drawdown stops behave differently in low-vol vs high-vol regimes.** A 5% drawdown in a 10-vol regime is 1-sigma noise; in a 25-vol regime, it's a quarter-sigma. Fixed-percentage drawdown stops therefore have very different signal value depending on the prevailing volatility regime, and become de-facto pro-cyclical (tighter stops in crash regimes when noise itself widens). The Cole / Artemis argument: vol-targeting and drawdown-stopping create their own feedback loops. (Source: [Cole — Volatility and the Allegory of the Prisoner's Dilemma])

44. **Calmar (return / max-drawdown) is the dominant metric for endowments and family offices, not Sharpe.** A family office with a -30% maximum-drawdown threshold (often hardcoded in the IPS) cannot use Sharpe-optimal portfolios that have empirically experienced -50% peaks. Calmar penalizes the realized worst-case path; Sharpe averages over volatility symmetrically. For LT money with hard floor constraints, Calmar-aware sizing is the operationally honest framework. (Sources: [Calmar Wikipedia]; [AQR — Should Your Portfolio Protection Work Fast or Slow])

45. **Anti-martingale (add to winners) is the pro practitioner default; martingale (add to losers) is dangerous outside very-high-edge mean-reversion contexts.** Pyramiding logic: each addition is funded "with the market's money" because the trailing stop is moved up to lock in prior gains. Martingale (averaging down) violates Kelly because it ignores the Bayesian update — the loser may have a deteriorated thesis you haven't priced. Druckenmiller, PTJ, Livermore, the Turtles, even Buffett's BRK accumulation are all anti-martingale. The exception: deep-value averaging-down is NOT martingale if each lower price is independently re-underwritten as a stronger edge case. (Sources: [QuantStrategy pyramiding]; [Astute Investor's Calculus martingale vs anti-martingale]; [Nial Fuller pyramid strategy])

## Section C (continued) — Additional disagreements

### C6. Full Kelly vs fractional Kelly — what fraction is empirically defensible?

- **Full Kelly camp (academic edge case)**: Maximizes long-run expected log-wealth; under known probabilities and infinite horizon, dominates all other strategies asymptotically.
- **Fractional Kelly camp (Thorp's own position post-Princeton-Newport)**: Half Kelly captures ~75% of the geometric growth at materially lower drawdown. Investors with finite horizons, parameter uncertainty, or career risk should size at half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly.
- **Resolution**: For discretionary equity PMs, the "true" edge is unknown and the probabilities estimated. Quarter-Kelly is defensible as the live-trading default; full Kelly is defensible only when (a) the edge is mechanical and re-measurable (statistical arb, MM-style edges) and (b) the horizon is multi-decade with no career-risk constraint. Discretionary stock-picker context: quarter to half is the empirical comfort zone.

### C7. Pod-shop disposition (mechanical drawdown stop) vs allocator-of-permanent-capital disposition (thesis-only)

- **Pod-shop view**: Drawdown stops are mechanical and non-negotiable. A 5% slow-bleed long-term hold with intact thesis still triggers the stop. Disposition is fundamentally constrained by the platform's risk-budget pacing, not the position's fundamentals.
- **Permanent-capital view (Buffett, Klarman, Pershing)**: Drawdown is irrelevant to the disposition decision; only the thesis matters. A 30% drawdown on intact-thesis name is an opportunity to add, not a stop.
- **The disagreement is structural, not philosophical**: Pod-shop PMs literally cannot run permanent-capital dispositions. The platform's leverage and pass-through fee structure both require constant capital-cycling. The two camps cannot be reconciled within the same vehicle — they require different investment vehicles.
- **Implication for an individual PM running their own book**: Choose the disposition philosophy AT VEHICLE FORMATION, because changing it later requires changing the capital base and the LP agreement.

### C8. Tax disposition — can after-tax math actually overrule fundamentals?

- **Munger's position (extended)**: Tax-deferral math (15% pre-tax, 35% terminal tax → 13.3% net vs 9.75% if paid annually) is so dominant that a true compounder should almost never be sold for non-thesis reasons. Same logic implies STCG → LTCG threshold delays should override mild thesis weakening near the 1-year mark.
- **Counter (Marks, Druckenmiller)**: Tax math is downstream of thesis. If the thesis breaks, sell — eat the tax. Anchoring on tax-deferral when the underlying is deteriorating is just the disposition effect dressed up in tax language.
- **Resolution**: Tax math IS load-bearing for "is the thesis still mostly intact" — i.e., it's correct to hold through mild-quality-decay until LTCG threshold. Tax math is NOT load-bearing for genuine thesis breaks. The line is drawn at the magnitude of the thesis update, not the magnitude of the tax differential.

### C9. Buffett's Apple trim — was tax-aware partial exit correct, or was it the disposition effect?

- **Tax-and-concentration view (Buffett's stated rationale)**: At ~40% of Berkshire's equity portfolio, AAPL became a concentration risk; locking in some gain at long-term rates was prudent.
- **Compounding-supremacy view (Buffett's later self-critique)**: "I sold it too soon." Versus 30-year AmEx and 35-year KO benchmarks, the AAPL trim looks like a concentration-fear-driven mistake.
- **Open empirical question**: Is there ANY documented case where Buffett's mid-thesis trim of a true compounder beat the hold-through? The KO/AmEx/AAPL case set suggests the answer is approximately no — i.e., "lethargy" is empirically the right disposition for genuine compounders even at extreme concentration. Marks's "trim on concentration" rule may be WRONG for the rare-compounder case.

### C10. Anti-martingale (add winners) vs deep-value averaging-down

- **Anti-martingale orthodoxy**: Druckenmiller, PTJ, Livermore, Turtles — never average down. Add only to positions showing open profit. Each addition is funded by trailing-stop slack.
- **Deep-value heterodoxy**: Buffett, Klarman, Marks — buy more as price falls IF the thesis re-confirms at the lower price. KO during 1990 mini-crash; airline-credit-after-2001 type opportunities.
- **Reconciliation**: The two camps disagree on what "averaging down" means. For trend-trader anti-martingale, adding-on-loss is mechanical (price-based, no re-underwriting) and ignores the negative Bayesian update. For value heterodoxy, adding-on-loss is contingent on a fresh thesis-confirmation pass at the new price — which is functionally an anti-martingale at the level of evidence rather than price. Both camps reject MECHANICAL martingale. The disagreement is whether price-decline-without-thesis-change is allowed to trigger more buying.

### Operating notes for skill builders (continued)

- **Sizing default for discretionary equity**: Quarter-Kelly to half-Kelly. Full Kelly is for known-probability mechanical edges, not discretionary stock-picking.
- **Drawdown-aware sizing**: For PMs serving allocators with a hard drawdown floor (endowments, family offices, pod-shop platforms), use Vince/Calmar-aware sizing rather than mean-variance.
- **Pod-shop disposition reality**: At a multi-strat pod, EVERY position is implicitly a swing because of the 5%/7.5% drawdown stops. Permanent-capital dispositions live at the firm's center book, not the pod level.
- **Tax-aware horizon shifting**: Default to defer past 1-year mark if thesis is mostly intact; do NOT defer if thesis is genuinely broken. The line is the magnitude of the thesis update, not the magnitude of the tax differential.
- **Wash-sale-aware harvesting**: Sell loser → buy non-substantially-identical proxy (sector ETF) → wait 31 days → swap back. This is a SCHEDULED non-fundamental disposition action that doesn't break the long-term thesis.
- **Evaluation-cadence discipline**: Watch P&L quarterly, not daily. Benartzi-Thaler evidence is direct: more frequent evaluation truncates equity exposure via myopic loss aversion.
- **Anti-martingale baseline**: Default to adding only on confirmation. Averaging down requires fresh thesis-re-underwriting at the lower price (Klarman/Buffett value variant), not mechanical price-based adds.
- **Buffett four-case lesson**: KO worked (durable moat held); IBM failed (broken moat held too long); airlines was process-correct under uncertainty; AAPL trim was self-acknowledged premature. The synthesis: revisit the thesis quarterly, not the price.
