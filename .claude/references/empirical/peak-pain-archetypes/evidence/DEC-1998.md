## A. Industry shock & operational stress  (industry_tailwind = structural-decline)

The minicomputer category — DEC's product franchise from PDP-1 (1960) and PDP-8 (1965) through VAX (1977) — was being structurally displaced by (1) workstations from Sun/HP/IBM, (2) commodity x86 PCs running Microsoft + Novell + later Linux, and (3) the broader Unix-on-RISC commoditization. This is `structural-decline` (single canonical spec value), not cyclical `weakening` — the entire VAX/VMS install base was being replaced, not pausing. Olsen's late-1980s resistance to the Unix/open-systems pivot is widely cited as the strategic failure that locked DEC into a declining category. Alpha (1992) was a credible technical response but arrived too late to outrun the platform shift.

## B. Balance sheet & cash position  (cash_runway = 12-24mo)

DEC reported $2.8B FY1992 loss; $339M FY1994 loss; $260.5M Q1-FY93 loss; recurring quarterly losses through FY1996. Layoffs of up to 15,000 announced 1992. The firm survived ~6 years from Olsen ouster to Compaq acquisition (Jun-1998 close, $9.6B largest computer-industry merger at the time), implying cash + ongoing service-revenue base were sufficient to sustain operations but not to fund a turnaround at peak burn. Spec value `12-24mo` (single canonical value) reflects the trough-window stress: distressed enough to force a sale but not month-to-month emergency. Not `>24mo` (recurring multi-billion-dollar losses), not `<12mo` or `distressed` (no Ch11 / capital-emergency event).

## C. Strategic context (NOT a universal-core feature)

Alpha 21064 (EV4) launched Nov-1992 at up to 192 MHz; AlphaStation/AlphaServer line 1994. Strong technical product, weak commercial traction vs Wintel/Sun. Palmer's 9-business-unit restructuring (1992+) and PC division divestiture failed to restore profitability. Compaq absorbed DEC's services arm to bolt onto its PC franchise; Compaq itself was acquired by HP in 2002.

## D. Founder/insider behavior  (founder_in_place = departed; founder_insider_stake_direction = departed)

Co-founders Ken Olsen + Harlan Anderson + Stan Olsen founded DEC 1957 in Maynard MA (former woolen mill) with $70K from American Research and Development Corp (Doriot) for a 70% share. Anderson departed decades earlier. Ken Olsen (President/CEO 1957-1992) was forced to resign by the Board in July 1992 after two years of operating-income losses — coincident with the $2B quarterly loss + 15,000-person layoff plan. Robert Palmer (CEO mid-1992 → 1998) was a competent professional manager but did not rescue the franchise. Spec values: `founder_in_place = departed` (Olsen ousted; Palmer is `replaced-by-competent` framing alternative — catalog primary anchor is Olsen-departed = the failure event, not Palmer's competence). `founder_insider_stake_direction = departed` accordingly.

## E. Margin & revenue trajectory  (margin_trajectory = deteriorating; revenue_trajectory = declining)

Per archival sources: FY1992 net loss $2.8B; FY1994 net loss $339M; brief Q4-FY93 profit ($113.2M) was the only positive quarter in the early-1990s window. From 1991-1996 DEC lost money every year but one. Headcount cut from peak ~140K (1989) to ~55K by sale (1998). Revenue trajectory `declining` (single canonical spec value); margin trajectory `deteriorating` (single canonical spec value).

## F. Operational shock specific to DEC — minicomputer obsolescence + delayed Unix pivot

DEC's strategic failure was Olsen's late-1980s public dismissal of Unix and open systems ("Unix is snake oil" attributed to Olsen, 1980s) at the exact moment when the IT industry was committing to that platform stack. Alpha was a tactical recovery; Palmer's restructuring was operational hygiene. Neither addressed the fact that the customer base was migrating off VAX faster than DEC could replace revenue. Compaq acquisition Jun-1998 = NON-SURVIVOR-as-independent (catalog framing).
