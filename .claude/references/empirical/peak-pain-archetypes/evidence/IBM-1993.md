## A. Industry shock & operational stress  (industry_tailwind = reversed)

WebSearch (Wikipedia/History of IBM): "the mainframe-centric corporation grappled with adapting swiftly to the burgeoning Unix open systems and personal computer revolutions, as desktop machines and Unix midrange computers emerged as cost-effective and easily manageable alternatives, overshadowing multi-million-dollar mainframes." The industry tailwind for IBM's core profit pool (System/370 and S/390 mainframes) was not just weakening — it was structurally reversed. Customers were actively migrating workloads OFF mainframes onto Sun/HP Unix boxes and Compaq/Dell PC servers. This is `reversed` rather than `weakening` because the secular tailwind that drove IBM's 1980s dominance had inverted: scale-up centralized computing was losing share to scale-out distributed computing.

NOT structural-decline (mainframes survived as a niche through 2026), but the tailwind that drove premium ASPs and 50%+ GM had reversed by 1992.

## B. Balance sheet & cash position  (cash_runway = 12-24mo)

WebSearch (UPI Archives 1993-01-19, "IBM posts worst year ever"): IBM announced a **$4.97 billion net loss for FY1992** with restructuring charges of $11.6 billion pre-tax. WebSearch (Wikipedia/John F. Akers): "The dividend was also slashed from $1.21 to 54 cents [quarterly], after the company had failed to make enough profit to cover its dividend payments for eight business quarters." Annualized: $4.84 → $2.16, a 55% dividend cut — the first IBM dividend cut since the Depression and a clear distress signal.

IBM still had material cash and an investment-grade balance sheet, BUT cash burn rate (Q4 1992 -$5.46B) and the magnitude of restructuring required (Gerstner ultimately took an additional $8.9B Q2 1993 pre-tax charge with 50,000 early retirements) push runway into 12-24mo at FY1992 close. Not "<12mo" (no liquidity crisis) but not ">24mo" (the dividend cut alone signals capital pressure).

→ **cash_runway = "12-24mo"** (single canonical value from spec ["<12mo","12-24mo",">24mo","distressed"]).

## C. Strategic context (NOT a universal-core feature; archetype-matching only)

Gerstner's services pivot (IBM Global Services launched 1996, growing to ~50% of revenue by 2000) is the canonical IBM turnaround playbook but is FY1994+ context. At FY1992 close the company had announced restructuring intentions but not the services pivot. Akers' final breakup-into-13-baby-blues plan was effectively reversed by Gerstner who decided to keep IBM integrated.

## D. Founder/insider behavior  (founder_in_place = replaced-by-competent; founder_insider_stake_direction = departed)

WebSearch (Wikipedia/John F. Akers): "On January 26, 1993... Akers announced his resignation after IBM's board insisted it had confidence in his leadership mere months earlier... He remained as CEO for three months while directors selected a successor, officially retiring on April 1, 1993." WebSearch (Wikipedia/History of IBM): "Lou Gerstner... succeeded Akers as both chairman and CEO on April 1, 1993—notably the first IBM CEO recruited from outside the company. IBM director Jim Burke orchestrated the management transition through secret meetings between rival Paul Rizzo and outsider Gerstner." Gerstner came from RJR Nabisco (and McKinsey/Amex before that), no IBM tenure — pure outsider. His subsequent track record (IBM stock from ~$12 split-adjusted at trough to ~$120+ by 1999) makes "competent" the unambiguous classification.

→ **founder_in_place = "replaced-by-competent"** (single canonical value; per era-specific operator note, Akers' replacement by Gerstner is the canonical "competent-outsider rescue" pattern).

Note: IBM's actual founder Thomas J. Watson Sr. died 1956; Watson Jr. retired 1971. Akers represented the inside-IBM career-CEO line (joined 1960, CEO 1985). His departure terminated that lineage. No insider stake increase by Akers in 1992 documented; he departed under board pressure with no rebuilding stake.

→ **founder_insider_stake_direction = "departed"** (single canonical value from ["increasing","flat","decreasing","departed"]).

## E. Margin & revenue trajectory  (margin_trajectory = deteriorating; revenue_trajectory = declining)

WebSearch (UPI 1993-01-19): IBM's Q4 FY1992 loss was $5.46 billion — the largest single-quarter loss in US corporate history at that time. WebSearch (Tech-Insider archive): "IBM's net loss of $4.97 billion for the year includes restructuring charges totaling $11.6 billion -- before taxes." Mix-shift away from high-margin mainframes (50%+ GM) toward low-margin PCs/services compressed company-wide GM materially.

→ **margin_trajectory = "deteriorating"** (single canonical spec value).

Revenue: FY1990 $69.0B → FY1991 $64.8B → FY1992 $64.5B. Multi-year revenue decline of ~6.5% from peak; mainframe segment specifically down sharply year-over-year as customers deferred S/390 purchases pending Gerstner's strategy clarification.

→ **revenue_trajectory = "declining"** (single canonical value from ["growing","flat","declining","pre-revenue"]).

## F. Operational shock specific to IBM — mainframe death-spiral (idiosyncratic)

The fundamental driver was a Christensen-style classic disruption: IBM's core profit pool (mainframes) was being eaten from below by Unix midrange + commodity PC servers running client-server applications. Akers' strategy was to break IBM into 13 independent units ("baby blues") so each could compete in its own market — board lost confidence in this approach. WebSearch (Deseret News 1993-07-27): Gerstner subsequently announced "an $8 billion second-quarter loss, including a huge charge for a corporate restructuring that includes the early retirement of 50,000 employees... another 35,000 employees to leave by the end of 1994... $8.9 billion pretax restructuring charge should produce annual savings of $4 billion." This July 1993 charge is recovery context, not FY1992-trough lens.
