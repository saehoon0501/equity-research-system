# L1 — Regime capture from cross-asset signals

Empirical knowledge library for identifying regime via rates, credit, FX, commodities, and equity-vol signals. Focus is on practitioner-relevant lead-lag relationships validated across 50+ years of data.

## Section B — Distilled patterns

1. **The 10y-3m Treasury spread has preceded every U.S. recession since 1960 with no false positives in that window.** Average lead is ~12 months, but the empirical range is 6-24 months across cycles, and equities often continued rising 12-18 months after initial inversion (sources: [1], [2], [32]).

2. **Estrella-Mishkin probability calibration is the practitioner-grade mapping.** Spread > +1.2pp ≈ <5% recession probability over next 12m; spread = 0 ≈ 25%; spread = -0.8pp ≈ 50%. Useful as a real-time scorecard rather than a binary flag (source: [1]).

3. **The near-term forward spread (NTFS = 6q-fwd minus 3m bill) dominates the 2-10 spread for forecasting both recession and 4-quarter equity returns.** When the two diverge — as in 2022 (2-10 inverted while NTFS did not) — NTFS gives the cleaner signal because it directly impounds expected Fed policy 12-18 months out (sources: [3], [4]).

4. **All recessionary forecasting power of credit spreads loads on the excess bond premium (EBP), not the default-risk component.** A 50bp rise in EBP → ~15pp rise in 12-month recession probability. The default-component contribution is statistically indistinguishable from zero (sources: [5], [6]).

5. **Credit spreads typically widen 2-8 weeks before equity peaks in an acute episode, and 3-9 months before recessions in a slower-moving cycle.** HY OAS broke 500bp in Sept-Nov 2007 (S&P peaked Oct 2007), and again in late Feb 2020 just ahead of the March SPX low. A widening of HY-OAS by ~300bp from recent low is the practitioner trigger (sources: [5], [6], [26]).

6. **EBP false positives are rare but real.** The most-cited is 2002 (Enron/WorldCom accounting scandals lifted credit spreads without producing a recession). This argues for using EBP alongside — not instead of — yield-curve signals (source: [6]).

7. **VIX is a coincident, not leading, indicator of equity drawdowns.** VIX peaked in late 2008 while SPX bottomed March 2009. VIX is best read as a regime *state* variable, not a turning-point predictor (source: VIX-SPX divergence research consensus + [24]).

8. **VIX term-structure backwardation is a regime *confirmation*, not a forward-return signal.** Backwardation has accompanied every SPX drawdown ≥10% since 2008, but unconditional forward returns following backwardation are not negative — many of the best forward returns occurred immediately *after* peak backwardation. Combining VIX level with curve slope reduces false-positive flags vs level alone (source: [24]).

9. **Bond volatility (MOVE) tends to lead equity volatility (VIX) in macro-driven regime shifts.** Bond markets price macro/policy risk earlier; cross-asset vol co-movement (VIX-MOVE) is itself a tradable signal for monthly bond and equity returns (per the volatility co-movement literature surfaced in research; primary published support: [13] on the funding-liquidity transmission mechanism that drives this lead).

10. **Term premium (ACM model) is countercyclical: it rises during stress / disagreement and compresses during calm.** This makes the term premium a regime-context variable, not a turning-point forecaster — useful for sizing rather than timing (source: [9]).

11. **The Cochrane-Piazzesi factor (tent-shaped combination of forward rates) forecasts bond excess returns with R² up to 0.44 and is countercyclical.** It also forecasts equity returns and long-run output, embedding it in the broader regime-signal stack (source: [7]).

12. **Cieslak-Povala cycle factor — yield variation orthogonal to expected inflation — outperforms forward-rate-only models for bond risk premia.** The decomposition formalizes that "rates regime" and "inflation regime" are separable signals (source: [8]).

13. **The copper-to-gold ratio leads the 10y Treasury yield by a few days at correlation ~0.85, but the relationship breaks around major macro shocks.** Use as a directional confirmation, not a primary signal. The signal degraded post-2020 with extraordinary monetary policy (source: [21]).

14. **The Sahm Rule (3m-MA unemployment ≥0.5pp above prior-12m low) has triggered in every U.S. recession since 1950 with one ambiguous false positive (1959).** Lead is typically zero — it identifies recessions ~3 months *into* them. Coincident, not leading, but more timely than NBER dating. Susceptible to supply-side false positives (source: [27]).

15. **The Conference Board LEI leads turning points by ~7 months on average, but is prone to recent false-positive episodes (2022-23).** The 3Ds rule (six-month diffusion ≤50 + ≤−4.3% six-month growth rate annualized) is the standard recession trigger (source: [28]).

16. **Chicago Fed NFCI leads economic activity by 1-2 months and is a 105-component weekly real-time read on financial-conditions tightness.** Positive readings = tighter than average; the index leads many narrower FCIs because it spans risk/credit/leverage simultaneously (source: [22]).

17. **Cross-asset value and momentum strategies share a global common factor structure tied to funding-liquidity risk.** Implication: when funding stress shows up (rising EBP, widening swap spreads, VIX/MOVE jointly elevated), expect simultaneous drawdowns in cross-asset carry, momentum, and risk-parity portfolios — i.e. classic risk-off (sources: [11], [13], [17]).

18. **VIX-driven carry-trade unwinds: a one-standard-deviation rise in VIX (when FX correlations also flip from low to high regime) corresponds to a measured ~34bp/day carry-trade drawdown.** Carry P&L is a high-frequency regime indicator for global risk appetite (source: [17]).

19. **Equity volatility and credit spreads are tied via Merton-style mechanics, but the link broke 2020-2022 (Fed liquidity compressed credit spreads disproportionately) and reconverged from 2022 onward.** Divergences between the two are themselves regime markers — when they decouple, look for non-fundamental drivers (Fed flow, regulatory, technical) (source: [25]).

20. **Most "predictors" in the equity-premium literature fail out-of-sample.** Goyal-Welch (and Goyal-Welch-Zafirov 2024) document that of 29 post-2008 candidate predictors plus the original 17, more than a third lose in-sample significance and half of the rest fail OOS. Yield-curve and credit-spread signals survive this test better than valuation-based predictors. Default treatment for any new "regime signal": demand OOS evidence and survival across multiple cycles (source: [10]).

21. **Dollar (DXY) is a regime *filter*, not a turning-point signal for U.S. equities.** Cleaner read: DXY relative to 200d MA. DXY-up regime → growth-leadership and EM headwinds; DXY-down regime → value leadership and EM/commodity tailwind. Dollar–S&P contemporaneous correlation is unstable (sign-flips across cycles) (source: practitioner consensus surfaced via DXY research review).

22. **Oil rate-of-change >+75% YoY has historically clustered close to recession onset (late-cycle indicator), but oil signals can lead, coincide, or lag equity peaks across cycles.** S&P 500 has typically peaked ~5 months before recessions while the GSCI has topped ~2 months *after* recession start, so commodities are not a reliable equity-peak leading indicator (source: [29]).

23. **Dollar regime is a global financial-conditions variable, not just an FX cross.** Offshore-dollar funding ($150bn/day eurodollar volumes, FBO branches >90% of borrowers) means USD strength = synthetic global tightening *outside* Fed policy. DXY rising 10%+ over 12m has empirically triggered EM funding stress in every cycle since the float (1980s LatAm debt crisis, 1997-98 Asia, 2013, 2014-16, 2018, 2022). The Fed's swap-line and FIMA-repo facilities exist *because* DXY transmits regime to non-US risk assets (sources: [33], [34], [35]).

24. **Triffin-paradox tension is currently dormant but not resolved.** Reserve currency status requires sustained US current-account deficits (to supply world dollars), but unbounded deficits eventually erode confidence. Setser's empirical point ([36]) is that even hypothetical 100% non-USD oil pricing would shift only 3-4% of global USD transactions — the dollar's regime-anchor role is sticky, but not unconditional. Implication for regime work: don't model "dollar collapse" tail risk as a base case, but track reserve-manager behavior and TIC flows for early signals.

25. **DXY cycle drives EM equity returns inversely with elasticity ~-1.5x to -2x in cyclical episodes.** EM-equity AUM moves inversely with DXY at near-coincident frequency; the channel is (a) USD-debt service costs for EM corporates/sovereigns, (b) commodity-price denomination effects for EM exporters, (c) capital-flow reallocation. The 2022 episode (DXY +12%, MSCI EM -22% USD) was textbook; the 2014-16 episode similarly wrenching for commodity-exporter EMs (sources: [33], [46]).

26. **DXY trend modulates US equity *style* leadership.** Strong-dollar regimes favor US large-cap growth (domestic-revenue, low-FX-translation drag) over US value/cyclicals/small-cap. Weak-dollar regimes favor commodity producers, multinationals, and EM. The 2017 weak-dollar year (-9% DXY) saw value/EM outperform; 2022 strong-dollar saw US large-cap growth outperform on a relative basis until tech rate-shock dominated. Use as relative-allocation signal, not absolute-direction signal (source: [36] + practitioner consensus on DXY-style relationships).

27. **March 2020 Treasury market dysfunction was the canonical "safe-asset broke" episode.** Mechanism: foreign-official selling + cash-futures basis-trade unwinds + dealer balance-sheet limits binding (post-Dodd-Frank, SLR-binding) → 10y yield rose during equity drawdown (the inverse of normal flight-to-quality). Resolved only by Fed direct purchases of $1tn+ Treasuries in two weeks. Regime lesson: dealer-intermediation capacity is itself a state variable; when it binds, cross-asset correlations flip (sources: [37], [38]).

28. **Cash-futures basis trade is a recurring regime-amplifier, not a one-time risk.** HF short-Treasury-futures positions rebuilt to ~$715bn by mid-2023 (just below 2019 peak) and reached ~$1tn-equivalent in Cayman holdings by end-2024 ([40]). Trigger for forced unwind is a repo-rate spike that pushes financing costs above arbitrage spread. The September 2019 repo episode (SOFR 2.43% → >5%) was a near-miss; March 2020 was the realized event. When MOVE is elevated *and* repo-IOER spread is widening, basis-trade tail risk is on (sources: [39], [40], [42], [43]).

29. **SLR-binding regimes mechanically reduce dealer Treasury-intermediation capacity in stress.** The April-2020 to March-2021 SLR exemption is the natural experiment: while in place, dealer Treasury holdings stayed elevated; expiration in March 2021 was followed by progressive thinning of dealer cash-Treasury liquidity. Track GSIB SLR proximity to 5% minimum as a regime input; when slack <50bp aggregate, expect basis blowouts and dysfunction risk in stress (source: [41]).

30. **SOFR-IOER (now SOFR-IORB) spread is a leading indicator of repo-market plumbing stress.** Pre-September 2019, the spread had widened on consecutive tax dates as reserves declined; the September 17 spike was preceded by warning signs. Persistent positive SOFR-IORB > +5bp signals reserve scarcity; spikes >50bp signal acute plumbing stress and pre-stage Fed intervention (sources: [42], [43]).

31. **Hamilton's oil-shock empirical: 9 of 10 post-WWII US recessions preceded by an oil-price spike, but causation is supply-side.** Demand-driven oil rises (e.g., 2003-07 China-driven price climb) are less recessionary than supply shocks of equivalent magnitude. Discriminate via the futures curve: backwardation + spot spike = supply shock (high recession risk); contango + spot rise = demand strength (low recession risk). The 2007-08 episode was supply-driven and contributed to recession; the 2021-22 episode was a hybrid (sources: [44], [45]).

32. **Backwardation vs contango is itself a regime-state read on commodity supply-demand balance.** Persistent oil-curve backwardation (front > deferred) = physical tightness, bullish near-term, often coincides with cycle-late expansions or supply shocks. Persistent contango = oversupply, bearish near-term, often coincides with cycle troughs. The shape leads convenience-yield-driven inventory builds/draws by 4-8 weeks; useful as cyclical-position read independent of price level (source: [44], [45] mechanism + practitioner trading literature).

33. **Copper-gold-ratio post-2020 break is structural, not transient.** Three concurrent drivers: (a) China's credit cycle decoupling — Beijing pivoted away from infrastructure-heavy credit impulse 2021+, breaking the historical China-credit → copper-demand link; (b) energy-transition copper demand (EV, grid, renewables) added a non-cyclical demand floor uncorrelated with US growth; (c) gold ETF flows became dominated by central-bank reserve-diversification rather than US-real-rates-driven private demand. The signal can be partially repaired by using copper alone vs 10y, but the gold-leg distortion is likely permanent (sources: existing [21] + commodity research consensus on China-credit and energy-transition demand).

34. **OPEC+ vs US shale break-even has moved from ~$70/bbl (2014) to ~$45/bbl (2018) to ~$60-65/bbl (2024) for incremental shale.** This sets a soft regime floor on oil prices in normal supply states; OPEC+ policy reactions trigger only when crude breaks ~$70 to the upside (raise quotas) or below shale break-even to the downside (cuts). Useful for sizing oil-equity positions: when WTI is mid-range ($55-75), expect OPEC+ inertia and supply-discipline mean-reversion; outside that band, expect policy regime shifts (source: [47]).

35. **2013 taper tantrum quantified the transmission of DM-rate-shock to EM.** EM FX fell 6% vs USD in four months post-Bernanke testimony; "Fragile Five" (Brazil, India, Indonesia, Turkey, South Africa) saw 2-3x the average drawdown. Vulnerability differentiation (current-account balance, reserves/imports, inflation-target credibility) explained ~70% of cross-country variance — fundamentals matter under stress, not under calm (sources: [46], [52]).

36. **EM crisis pattern across 1997, 1998, 2008, 2013, 2018, 2020, 2022: DM funding tightening → DXY rises → EM dollar-debtors face cash-flow and rollover stress → forced FX selling → currency overshoot → equity drawdown amplifies via foreign-investor unwind.** Average peak-to-trough EM-equity (USD) drawdown in DM-tightening cycles: ~25-40%. Recovery is often slow because credibility-rebuilding (FX reserves, fiscal adjustment, IMF programs) takes 12-24 months (sources: [46], [49], [50]).

37. **Reinhart's 200-year panel finding: capital-inflow bonanzas precede default and sudden-stop episodes with multi-year lead.** A 3-year run of inflows >2% of GDP raises sudden-stop probability ~3x baseline; the mechanism is balance-sheet leverage and currency-mismatch buildup that cannot survive an exogenous DM-rate shock. Use as a structural, slow-moving regime input on EM allocation timing (source: [50]).

38. **Bridgewater 4-box framework operationalized: equal-risk weight to each of (rising/falling growth) × (rising/falling inflation).** Asset mapping: rising growth + rising inflation → commodities, EM equity, inflation-linked bonds; rising growth + falling inflation → DM equity, corporate credit; falling growth + falling inflation → nominal bonds; falling growth + rising inflation (stagflation) → gold, commodities, defensive duration. The framework's empirical value is forcing explicit regime-conditional sizing — even discretionary investors benefit from the 4-box discipline as a checklist (sources: [23], [38] for asset-class mapping cross-checks).

39. **Post-COVID Fed reaction function is empirically different from pre-2020.** Bauer-Pflueger-Sunderam ([48]) document that the inflation-coefficient in markets' implied policy rule rose materially after March 2022 liftoff; rate-data sensitivity to inflation-surprise data ~doubled. Cleveland Fed ([53]) confirms independently. Implication for regime work: pre-2020 yield-curve and term-premium calibrations may understate Fed reactivity to inflation in current cycle — bias estimates accordingly. The 2019 inversion / 2020 recession sequence may be the last "clean" pre-regime-shift validation of the 10y-3m signal (sources: [48], [53]).

40. **CTA / trend-follower P&L is itself a coincident regime signal.** Trend strategies require sustained price moves; CTA drawdowns cluster in transition / range-bound regimes; CTA strong years (2014, 2022) coincide with persistent macro-driven trends. Track aggregate CTA performance (e.g., SG Trend Index) as a regime-classifier: -10%+ drawdowns over 6m signal regime-transition / mean-reversion; +15%+ runs signal trending macro regime. Not predictive, but useful as confirmation (source: practitioner consensus on trend-following + [11] cross-asset momentum literature).

41. **Practitioner regime-classification frameworks share a 4-state core but disagree on observability.** Bridgewater's growth × inflation 4-box ([23]) uses surveys + market-implied; macro hedge funds (Brevan Howard-style) use Taylor-rule deviations + financial-conditions indices; CTAs use price-trend persistence directly. The convergence in practice: every robust framework decomposes regime into (a) growth direction, (b) inflation direction, (c) financial-conditions tightness, (d) liquidity/funding state — even if labels differ. Single-factor frameworks (e.g., "is yield curve inverted") underperform 4-factor frameworks empirically (source: cross-source synthesis [23], [38], [22], [33]).

## Section C — Open questions / disagreements

1. **2-10 vs near-term-forward spread.** Engstrom & Sharpe argue NTFS dominates 2-10 ([3], [4]). Bauer & Mertens argue the choice of spread doesn't matter much — all yield-curve measures perform similarly ([2]). The 2022 episode was a real-world test (2-10 inverted, NTFS did not; no recession followed in 2023) which the NTFS camp claims as vindication, but the 2024-25 economic ambiguity leaves the verdict unsettled.

2. **Is the yield-curve signal still operative after QE/QT?** Some researchers argue compressed term premia from large-scale asset purchases mechanically distort the spread and that the 2019 inversion / pandemic-recession sequence was lucky timing. Others (Bauer & Mertens [2]) find the signal robust through 2018. The 2022-23 inversion-without-recession is the central piece of contradicting evidence.

3. **VIX backwardation: predictive or just confirmatory?** Cboe's own analysis ([24]) finds backwardation does *not* reliably predict subsequent negative returns outside outright crises. Other practitioner sources treat it as a regime trigger. The empirical question — does conditioning forward-return strategies on backwardation add alpha after fees — remains contested.

4. **Credit-equity divergence interpretations.** When credit spreads tighten while equity vol stays elevated (or vice versa), some argue this is a Fed-flow distortion ([25]) and the underlying signal is unchanged; others argue the divergence itself is the actionable signal because it reveals which market is being mispriced. No agreed-upon adjudication.

5. **Copper-gold ratio post-2020.** The historical 0.85 correlation with 10y yields has weakened materially since 2021 ([21]). Whether this is a temporary structural break (China stimulus dynamics, gold ETF flows, energy-transition copper demand) or a permanent decay of the signal is unresolved.

6. **Sahm Rule supply vs demand sensitivity.** The Sahm Rule triggered in 2024 but Claudia Sahm herself argued the 2024 trigger was driven by labor-supply growth (immigration) rather than demand collapse — a regime mismatch. The rule's historical reliability may be conditional on demand-driven unemployment cycles ([27]).

7. **Adaptive-markets implication for any signal.** Per Lo ([18]), risk-reward relationships are not stable over time; signals decay as they're arbitraged. This puts a cap on how much weight any single regime signal should bear. Practitioners disagree on how much decay to assume — Marks ([19]) emphasizes recurring patterns of greed/fear; quants like AQR build in factor-decay assumptions explicitly.

8. **Single-factor regime models vs multi-factor frameworks.** Bridgewater's growth × inflation 4-box ([23]) is parsimonious; Hamilton-style HMM models with multiple latent states ([15]) fit data better but are prone to overfitting. The practitioner / academic gap on regime granularity remains open.

9. **Causation vs correlation in yield-curve recessions.** Bauer & Mertens explicitly flag this ([2]) — does an inverted curve *cause* a recession (via tight monetary policy compressing credit) or merely *signal* market participants' aggregate expectations? Different causal stories imply different operational use.

10. **EBP signal in low-volatility regimes.** The 2002 false positive ([6]) suggests the EBP can fire on idiosyncratic credit shocks (corporate-governance scandals) that don't generalize. No consensus on filtering these out ex-ante.

11. **Has the post-COVID Fed regime invalidated pre-2018 yield-curve calibrations?** Bauer-Pflueger-Sunderam ([48]) and Cleveland Fed ([53]) document that markets' perceived Fed reaction function changed materially post-March 2022. If Fed inflation-reactivity is structurally higher now, recession lead times from yield-curve signals may compress (faster transmission) or the signal may fire on inflation-fighting policy that doesn't ultimately tip the economy. The 2022-25 episode is consistent with both interpretations and the verdict is unsettled.

12. **Pozsar's "Bretton Woods III" thesis vs Setser's reserve-stickiness view.** Pozsar ([35]) argues sanctions weaponization has accelerated reserve diversification toward commodities/gold and away from US Treasuries. Setser ([36]) counters that the empirical magnitude of reserve diversification remains small (low single-digit % of global USD transactions). The disagreement matters for regime work: if Pozsar is right, term premium and dollar-funding dynamics will structurally shift; if Setser is right, current pre-2022 calibrations remain valid. No clean resolution yet.

13. **Is the basis trade a destabilizer or a stabilizer of Treasury markets?** [40] documents Cayman HF basis-trade absorbs ~37% of net Treasury issuance — without this demand, yields would be materially higher. But the 2020 episode shows the trade unwinds violently in stress. The regulatory debate (CFTC, FSOC, BPI vs SIFMA) has not converged on whether basis-trade activity should be limited or whether dealer balance sheets should be expanded (SLR reform) to absorb the unwind risk. Either resolution changes Treasury-market regime dynamics.

14. **Does dollar regime cause EM stress or merely correlate with it?** Identification problem: DXY rises typically coincide with Fed tightening and risk-off. Disentangling dollar-funding-channel (FBO branches, FX swap pricing) from rate-channel (USD borrowing-cost rise) and risk-channel (global VIX) is empirically hard. IMF [46] and Setser [36] emphasize the funding channel; rate-focused models emphasize the rate channel. Practitioner implication: don't double-count by treating DXY and Fed tightening as independent regime inputs in the same cycle.

15. **Energy-transition vs cyclical-demand split for copper.** Bull case: structural EV/grid demand creates a non-cyclical floor and amplifies cyclical upside. Bear case: still ~50% Chinese property/construction demand, so until that recovers, cyclical drag dominates structural tailwind. The 2022-25 price action (range-bound despite supply deficits forecast) has not fully resolved the question. Implications for using copper-gold as a regime signal: until structural-demand share clearly exceeds cyclical-demand share, treat the signal as more noise-prone than pre-2020.

## Notes on methodology

- "Confidence" reflects the joint criteria of: (a) cross-cycle replication (≥3 cycles), (b) survival of out-of-sample tests where available, (c) practitioner adoption with verifiable track record.
- The yield-curve and credit-spread signals are the two strongest survivors of the Goyal-Welch-Zafirov 2024 ([10]) OOS audit and dominate the table accordingly.
- VIX-based signals are demoted from "leading" to "coincident" or "regime-state" status — VIX confirms regime, it does not predict it.
- Practitioner sources (Marks, Bridgewater, AQR, Pozsar, Setser) are used for *frameworks*; academic sources are used for *empirical magnitudes and lead times*.
- Treasury-market microstructure (SLR, basis trade, repo plumbing) is treated as a regime *state* variable rather than a turning-point predictor: when binding, cross-asset correlations flip and standard signals degrade.
- Post-COVID regime question is flagged but not resolved — pre-2018 calibrations of yield-curve / credit / Fed-reaction-function signals are presumed valid until contradicted, but with widened uncertainty bands per Bauer-Pflueger-Sunderam ([48]) and Cleveland Fed ([53]).
- Dollar regime (DXY) elevated to a primary regime input alongside yield curve and credit spreads, given the offshore-dollar funding architecture documented in [33]-[35]; this is a structural addition relative to the original lane file.
