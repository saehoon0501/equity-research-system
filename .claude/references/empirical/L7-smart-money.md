# L7 — Institutional flow / smart-money tracking: what survives empirical scrutiny

**Lane purpose.** Rank signals about what big institutional investors are doing by empirical edge confidence. The operator's working framework: at sub-$1M individual scale, ride-along on institutional accumulation during drawdowns/flats with reasonable valuation works (Lakonishok-Shleifer-Vishny 1994 contrarian logic); pure 13F replication during rallies doesn't (low edge after the 45-day lag). The "don't-work" set is as load-bearing as the "do-work" set — telling the operator what to ignore is half the value here.

**Operator modes referenced below:**
- **B** — drawdown/flat re-underwrite of a known name
- **B'** — drawdown/flat new initiation
- **C** — small/mid-cap or off-radar coverage
- **cross-mode** — useful in any mode as a confirming filter

## Section B — Distilled patterns

### B.1 — DO survive empirical scrutiny

- **Opportunistic insider purchases dominate routine trades.** Cohen-Malloy-Pomorski filter (drop predictable-pattern insiders) leaves a set earning ~82bp/month value-weighted, ~180bp/month equal-weighted; routine traders earn ~zero. Roughly half the universe is filtered out. The annualized ~10%+ abnormal return on the value-weighted opportunistic portfolio is the single highest-confidence smart-money signal in this lane. (Sources: Cohen-Malloy-Pomorski JF 2012; AQR write-up; Harvard CorpGov.)

- **Insider purchases > insider sales for predictability.** Lakonishok-Lee 1975–1995: aggregate insider activity is informative, but informativeness comes from *purchases* — sales reflect liquidity/diversification. Skip the noise of insider sells; weight buys. (Source: Lakonishok-Lee RFS 2001.)

- **Insider signal strongest in small firms.** Lakonishok-Lee: predictive power concentrates in smaller-cap names where information asymmetry is highest. Maps directly to operator's C-mode. (Sources: Lakonishok-Lee; 2iQ practitioner review.)

- **Cluster buying ~2x as informative as single-insider buys.** 21-day abnormal returns of ~3.8% for clustered purchases vs ~2.0% non-cluster; 2+ unique insiders within a short window (commonly 10 days) is the operative threshold. (Source: 2iQ review summarizing Alldredge-Blank, Kang-Kim-Wang.)

- **Filter out 10b5-1 planned trades.** Routine/scheduled trades under 10b5-1 plans are exactly the "no-information" pool Cohen-Malloy-Pomorski strip out. Operationally: only count Form 4 "P"-coded open-market purchases not under a 10b5-1 plan. (Sources: Cohen-Malloy-Pomorski; 2iQ review.)

- **Activist 13D filings produce ~7% abnormal return in (-20,+20) window.** Brav-Jiang-Partnoy-Thomas: ~2% on filing day, drifting to ~7.2% over 20 trading days. Bebchuk-Brav-Jiang follow-up shows no long-run reversal — gain is durable. (Source: Brav et al. JF 2008; March 2019 update.)

- **Activist 13D edge has compressed over time.** Average abnormal return fell from ~15.9% (2001) to ~3.4% (2006) as activism became crowded — consistent with McLean-Pontiff post-publication decay (~35–50%). The signal still works; the magnitude is shrinking. (Sources: Brav et al.; McLean-Pontiff.)

- **"Best Ideas" — top conviction position outperforms by 2.8–4.5%/yr with no reversal.** Across 1000+ active managers and 37+ years of data, the *single highest-conviction position* of an active manager beats both the rest of their book and the benchmark. Concentration matters; the dilution is what kills aggregate fund alpha. (Source: Antón-Cohen-Polk; Alpha Theory update.)

- **Mutual-fund fire sales create predictable, exploitable price pressure.** Coval-Stafford: trading against forced sellers (or buyers) earns highly significant returns. The signal is opposite-direction — it's about identifying when a stock is mechanically depressed by flow rather than fundamentals. Maps directly to B'-mode entries. (Source: Coval-Stafford JFE 2007.)

- **Behavioral foundation for "buy during drawdowns" is solid.** LSV 1994 establishes the value/contrarian premium isn't a risk premium — it's a behavioral premium from extrapolation errors. This is the empirical license for the operator's "ride along on institutional accumulation during drawdowns" framework. (Source: LSV JF 1994.)

- **Genuine unusual options activity (pre-publicity) predicts returns.** Pan-Poteshman buyer-initiated put-call ratios: ~40bp next-day, ~1%+ next-week predictability driven by non-public information. Easley-O'Hara-Srinivas establishes the theoretical channel: informed traders prefer leverage in options markets, particularly for small/mid where the leverage advantage is largest. (Sources: Pan-Poteshman RFS 2006; Easley-O'Hara-Srinivas JF 1998.)

### B.2 — DON'T survive empirical scrutiny

- **Pure 13F replication of large-cap rallies has near-zero net edge.** GURU underperformed S&P by ~0.5%/yr since 2016 and ~1.3%/yr over 2012–2021; ALFA −1.6%/yr same window. CFA Institute concludes copycat ETFs are "fair-weather investments that don't perform over an entire cycle." The 45-day lag erodes the edge in liquid large caps where price discovery is fast. (Sources: CFA Institute / Enterprising Investor; Quantpedia.)

- **High-turnover fund 13F cloning is structurally broken.** By the time the filing is public, the position may be exited. Faber: "Many funds, such as the hugely successful Renaissance Technologies, are not appropriate for trading due to their heavy involvement in derivatives." Quantpedia: "value of portfolio information relating to high-turnover funds has long since expired." Selectively cloning low-turnover fundamental managers is the only version that survives. (Sources: Meb Faber 2008; Quantpedia; Lancaster Systematic 13F paper.)

- **CNBC-publicized "unusual options activity" actively destroys edge.** Jiang-Strong: pre-publicity UOA predicts returns; CNBC coverage triggers immediate overreaction followed by significant *reversal* and negative long-run cumulative abnormal returns. Following the Najarian segment is empirically a losing strategy. (Source: Jiang-Strong SSRN 2020; Institutional Investor write-up.)

- **Sell-side analyst initiations have weak, transient effect.** Womack 1996: ~3% size-adjusted return on buy initiation, ~+2.4% modest 6-month drift; ~−4.7% on sells with much larger ~−9.1% 6-month drift. The buy-initiation drift is small enough that after costs and risk-adjustment it's not a durable trade thesis — though *sell* initiations are more meaningful (asymmetry consistent with short-sale frictions). (Source: Womack JF 1996.)

- **Average institutional trade is not informative — only specific subsets are.** Jegadeesh-Tang: institutions in aggregate do not earn abnormal profits around takeovers; only those whose broker is the target's advisor do (i.e., information asymmetry is the source, not skill). Yan-Zhang: long-term institutions' trading does *not* forecast returns; only short-term institutional trading does, and only in small/growth subsets. "Watch what institutions do" without further conditioning is folklore. (Sources: Jegadeesh-Tang 2010; Yan-Zhang RFS 2009; Gompers-Metrick.)

- **"Whale-watching" individual PMs without entry conditions is biographical entertainment.** Tracking Buffett or Klarman's full 13F without a valuation/drawdown filter ignores that the operator can't replicate their cost basis or holding horizon. Faber's own backtest found the strategy needs *low-turnover, fundamental* managers AND a holdings filter; without filters, the mean copycat ETF underperforms. (Sources: Faber 2008; CFA Institute Guru analysis.)

- **Single-insider, non-clustered Form 4 buys are weak.** Without clustering (2+ insiders, short window), the signal-to-noise ratio collapses. Lakonishok-Lee already showed aggregate insider buying matters more than individual; Cohen-Malloy-Pomorski's filter is necessary but not sufficient — clustering adds the second leg. (Sources: Lakonishok-Lee; 2iQ review.)

### B.3 — Conditional / regime-dependent

- **Best-Ideas premium has compressed post-2008.** Original 2.8–4.5%/yr is the long-sample number; post-2008 the gap narrowed and disappeared for hedge funds (mutual funds still show it). McLean-Pontiff predicts ~35% decay; the empirical pattern is consistent. Use it as *one input*, not standalone alpha. (Sources: Antón-Cohen-Polk; Quantpedia review; McLean-Pontiff.)

- **13F cloning works conditionally on manager type and regime.** Survives scrutiny only when (a) low-turnover fundamental manager, (b) entry filter on stock-level price/valuation, (c) drawdown/flat regime not late-cycle rally. The operator's framework — ride-along during drawdowns/flats — aligns with the conditions where it works; the failure mode is using it during rallies. (Sources: Faber 2008; Quantpedia; CFA Institute.)

- **13G new 5%-holder filings underexplored academically; signal value is plausible but unverified.** A QII or passive investor crossing 5% is publicly disclosed; in C-mode (small-caps) where 13F coverage is thin, this can be the first publicly observable institutional vote. Academic literature is much thinner here than for 13D — practitioner case for monitoring is reasonable; quantitative edge claims should be discounted absent direct study. (Sources: Brav et al. activism work for 13D context; SEC schedule definitions.)

## Section C — Open questions / disagreements

1. **Carhart vs Wermers on whether stock-picking skill exists.** Carhart 1997 attributes mutual-fund persistence to four-factor loadings, expenses, and transaction costs (i.e., no real skill). Wermers 2000 and DGTW 1997 find a ~1.3% gross-of-cost characteristic-based stock-picking premium. Reconciliation: skill exists at the individual-position level (the "Best Ideas") but is diluted at the fund level by closet-indexing and asset-gathering incentives.

2. **Does the Brav et al. activism premium reflect ex-ante target selection or ex-post operational improvement?** Brav et al. argue both — announcement-window returns capture market expectation of governance change; long-run returns + improved operating performance + higher CEO turnover capture realized changes. Critics (e.g., long-run firm-value debates in the Wachtell-Lipton vs Bebchuk dispute) question whether long-run effects are causal or selection. Operationally: 13D filings by *known* successful activists with operational track records carry the most signal.

3. **Gompers-Metrick (level matters) vs Yan-Zhang (only short-term institutional changes matter) vs DGTW characteristic decomposition.** Three different lenses on "do institutions add value." All three are partially right — aggregate institutional ownership is a demand-shock proxy (Gompers-Metrick), changes are noisy at the aggregate but informative for short-horizon institutions (Yan-Zhang), and characteristic-based skill does exist but is small (DGTW). Operator implication: *change* in ownership for a *specific* institution type is the meaningful signal — not the level, not the aggregate.

4. **Is the 45-day 13F lag fatal or partial?** Lancaster "Systematic 13F Hedge Fund Alpha" reports +3.80% S&P-relative for fundamental-equity-fund consensus despite the lag. CFA Institute analysis of ETF wrappers (GURU/ALFA) shows underperformance. Reconciliation: edge exists on *paper* with appropriate manager filtering, but ETF-wrapper implementation costs (fees, rebalancing, manager selection drift) eat the alpha in practice. A self-implemented ride-along on hand-picked low-turnover managers is empirically distinct from buying GURU.

5. **Cluster threshold — what counts as a "cluster"?** Practitioner sources use 2+ insiders within 10 days (or 3+, varying); academic studies use various definitions. No canonical empirical threshold. Operator should pre-commit a definition (e.g., 2+ insiders, ≥$50K each, P-coded, within 10 days, no 10b5-1 plan) to avoid hindsight selection.

6. **Options-flow signal in the era of 0DTE and meme-flow noise.** Pan-Poteshman 2006 was estimated on a much cleaner options market. Whether buyer-initiated put-call ratios still carry the same predictive content given today's retail options volume is an open empirical question. Easley-O'Hara-Srinivas channel (informed prefer leverage) plausibly remains; magnitude is uncertain.
