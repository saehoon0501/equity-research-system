# Practitioner regime-classification methodologies

**Scope.** What named macro-fund practitioners with verifiable track records *actually* use for regime classification — not what the literature attributes to them. Primary-source preferred (their own books, letters, interviews); verified secondary where primary is paywalled or inaccessible.

**Operator question driving this.** Existing references default to Bridgewater's 2-axis 4-box (growth × inflation). Should the system add a third axis (monetary policy / liquidity)? What other dimensions are practitioner-validated? This file answers by surveying named practitioners.

**Anti-hallucination.** Every URL in Section A was fetched or surfaced via WebSearch. Practitioner statements are quoted with link to primary or verified secondary source. Where the primary PDF could not be parsed (Bridgewater PDFs, Dalio PDF, Soros OSF transcript), I fall back to multiple verified secondary summaries that are mutually corroborating, and label as Tier 2.

## Section C — Cross-practitioner synthesis

### C.1 — Common dimensions across practitioners (consensus set)

Across the 8 practitioners surveyed, four dimensions show up *repeatedly*:

| Dimension | Bridgewater | Dalio (HtEMW) | Druckenmiller | Soros | Tepper | Marks | PTJ | Brevan H. |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Growth** (real activity vs trend/expectations) | **Yes** (axis 1) | Yes (productivity + STDC) | Yes (margin / 18–24m visualization) | Yes (in trend definition) | **Yes** (economy strengthens?) | Yes (economic + profit cycle) | Yes (macro filter) | Yes |
| **Inflation** (price level vs target/expectations) | **Yes** (axis 2) | Yes (in STDC) | Yes (rates / breakevens) | Implicit | Yes (Fed reaction) | Indirect (via credit) | Yes | Yes |
| **Monetary policy / liquidity** (CB balance sheet, real rates, MP-regime) | **Yes via Prince's MP1/2/3** | **Yes** (STDC core) | **Yes — primary lens** | Yes (rate-defense as reflexivity trigger) | **Yes** (Fed stance is core dimension) | Implicit (in credit cycle) | Yes (CB action filter) | Yes |
| **Credit / risk-appetite** (spreads, lender willingness, covenant quality) | Implicit (in growth-falling cell) | **Yes** (LTDC core) | Sometimes | **Yes** (forced liquidation = bust) | **Yes** (distressed credit origin of fund) | **Yes — most volatile cycle** | Indirect | Yes |

**Convergence finding.** The 4 dimensions that *every practitioner uses in some form* are: **Growth, Inflation, Monetary policy / liquidity, Credit / risk-appetite.** Bridgewater's public 2-axis is the *minimum* — every practitioner who has been pressed for detail (Prince, Druckenmiller, Marks, Tepper) adds at least one of {monetary policy, credit}.

### C.2 — Where practitioners disagree (idiosyncratic dimensions)

| Idiosyncratic dimension | Practitioners who use it | Practitioners who don't |
|---|---|---|
| **Investor psychology / sentiment** (greed-fear, "how much optimism priced in") | **Marks** (cycle 3); **Soros** (bias side of reflexivity) | Bridgewater All Weather, PTJ, Tepper (not explicit) |
| **Real-estate cycle as a separate axis** | **Marks** (cycle 6) | Everyone else |
| **Reflexivity / feedback loops** (price → fundamentals causation) | **Soros** (entire framework); Druckenmiller (in 1992 ERM short) | Bridgewater (deliberately rejected — All Weather assumes prices are mean-reverting around fundamentals) |
| **Currency / dollar regime as primary axis** | **Druckenmiller** (one of the four asset-class lenses); **Soros** | Bridgewater (subsumed in growth/inflation), Marks, Tepper |
| **Pure-price overlay (200d MA)** | **PTJ**, Druckenmiller (uses technicals as confirmation) | Marks ("we never know what's going to happen"), Bridgewater (rejects timing) |
| **8-stage sequencing** (where in a boom-bust we are) | **Soros**; partial in **Marks** (early-mid-late cycle) | Bridgewater (no sequencing — ambient classification), Druckenmiller |
| **Long-term debt cycle (50–75y)** | **Dalio** specifically (HtEMW) | Everyone else (operate at shorter horizons) |
| **Convexity / payoff asymmetry as substitute for regime call** | **Brevan Howard**; **Tepper** (2010 binary) | Marks (sizing-based), Bridgewater (sizing-based) |

### C.3 — The "minimum viable" practitioner set

If the system has to pick *one* practitioner framework, the empirical-confidence ranking is:

1. **Bridgewater 4-box** — most documented, longest live track record (1996–present), most widely replicated by other allocators (ALLW, RPAR, etc.). Its known limitation is no monetary-policy axis.
2. **Druckenmiller 3-lens (liquidity / valuation / technicals)** — specifically corrects Bridgewater's missing monetary-policy axis; track record is the strongest of any individual macro investor (~30%/yr for 30 years, no down year at Duquesne).
3. **Marks 6-cycle** — most thoroughly written-up; weakest on trading the regime, strongest on positioning posture.

**Minimum viable for an *equity research* system (which is the operator's use case):** Bridgewater 2-axis + a Druckenmiller-style monetary-policy/liquidity axis = a **3-axis framework** (growth × inflation × monetary-policy).

### C.4 — The "comprehensive" practitioner set

For a system that wants to maximize coverage:

- **Growth** (Bridgewater axis 1)
- **Inflation** (Bridgewater axis 2)
- **Monetary policy / liquidity** (Prince MP-regime, Druckenmiller balance-sheet trajectory)
- **Credit / risk-appetite** (Marks credit cycle, Tepper credit-distressed origin)
- **Investor psychology / sentiment** (Marks cycle 3, Soros bias)
- **Price / trend overlay** (PTJ 200d MA, Druckenmiller technical confirmation)

Six axes is what *the union of practitioner frameworks* covers. Above six is gold-plating.

## Section D — Specific findings

### D.1 — 2-axis (growth × inflation) vs 3-axis (+ monetary policy): which is better validated?

**Verdict: 3-axis is better validated empirically, but not by a wide margin in normal regimes — and by a *very* wide margin in regimes where monetary policy is constrained or coordinated with fiscal policy.**

Evidence:

- **Bridgewater itself, through Bob Prince, added the third axis** when MP-regime mattered. The "[Monetary Policy 3 — fiscal-monetary coordination](https://www.bridgewater.com/research-and-insights/bob-prince-on-monetary-policy-3-inflation-diversification-between-east-and-west)" framing is Bridgewater conceding that the 4-box alone is incomplete in MP-constrained regimes (zero lower bound, debt-monetization). This is the single strongest practitioner endorsement of the 3-axis position.
- **Druckenmiller has stated explicitly** that liquidity / monetary policy is the *primary* lens, with growth and inflation downstream: "Earnings don't move the overall market; it's the Federal Reserve Board… focus on the central banks and focus on the movement of liquidity… most people in the market are looking for earnings and conventional measures. It's liquidity that moves markets." Source: [Macro Ops notes / 2018 Real Vision](https://macro-ops.com/stanley-druckenmiller-on-liquidity-macro-margins/).
- **Macrosynergy's quantamental research** uses three dimensions explicitly: growth+inflation, monetary stance / money-credit conditions, and market-stress. Their published backtests show monetary-stance information adds tradable signal beyond the growth/inflation pair. Source: [Macrosynergy: Classifying market regimes](https://macrosynergy.com/research/classifying-market-regimes/) and [The (hidden) trading value of central bank liquidity information](https://macrosynergy.com/research/the-hidden-trading-value-of-central-bank-liquidity-information/).
- **Empirical academic clustering studies** (e.g., AlphaArchitect data-driven GMM regimes, Invesco DMFS, NBER WP 21863) consistently find that adding policy-rate / yield-curve dimensions to growth+inflation produces **distinguishable clusters that the 2-axis collapses** — most clearly the "low growth + low inflation + steep curve" (Goldilocks) vs. "low growth + low inflation + inverted curve" (recession-priced) distinction, which the Bridgewater 4-box puts in the same cell despite very different optimal asset weights.
- **The trade-off: cost of complexity.** Bridgewater chose 2-axis deliberately because (a) it's simpler to communicate, (b) risk-parity sizing on 4 cells is cleaner than on 8, and (c) MP-effects are partially absorbed into the growth/inflation surprises. The 3-axis framework requires more data, more rules for cell-transitions, and more potential for overfitting. For institutional risk-parity allocations, 2-axis may be the right operational choice. For *active equity trading where mispricing of MP-policy expectations is the alpha source*, 3-axis is mandatory.

**Operator recommendation.** For an equity research system targeting active stock-picking, use **3-axis: growth × inflation × monetary policy / liquidity**. Optionally add credit/risk-appetite as a 4th axis (Marks's most-volatile cycle); this picks up regimes that the 3-axis misses (e.g., 2008-style credit-cycle terminations where growth/inflation/policy all look fine but spreads are the leading signal).

### D.2 — What dimensions does Druckenmiller add to the 4-box?

Two principal additions:

1. **Liquidity / central-bank balance-sheet trajectory** — *rate of change*, not level. The transition from "$1T/yr CB buying → 0" was, in his 2018 Real Vision framing, the most important macro variable of the cycle. Source: [Real Vision 2018](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BcrFzL5bOqM); [Macro Ops notes](https://macro-ops.com/notes-druckenmiller-real-vision-interview/).
2. **Currency / FX regime as a co-equal asset class.** Druckenmiller's "272 charts nightly" cover every currency cross — currencies are not a derivative of growth/inflation in his framework; they are an independent regime axis (e.g., his 2026 dollar-bearishness is structural-purchasing-power-based, not growth/inflation-derived). Source: [Hard Lessons](https://www.morganstanley.com/insights/videos/hard-lessons/duquesne-stan-druckenmiller-iliana-bouzali); [RealClearPolitics 2026](https://www.realclearpolitics.com/video/2026/03/01/druckenmiller_portfolio_no_longer_ai-driven_bearish_on_dollar_at_historic_purchasing_power_peak.html).

Secondary additions: **technicals / price-confirmation** (he won't take a position fundamentals say is right if price disagrees) and **profit-margin trajectory at 18–24-month horizon** (the future state that current price doesn't reflect).

### D.3 — What does Soros add? (Reflexivity-driven feedback)

Soros adds **reflexivity** as a *meta-dimension* that overlays any growth/inflation/policy classification:

- **Causation runs both ways.** Asset prices don't just reflect fundamentals; they *change* fundamentals (high stock prices → easier credit ratings → real fundamental improvement → reinforces price). Bridgewater's All Weather *deliberately rejects* this — its risk-parity assumes prices are noisy reflections of underlying environments.
- **The 8-stage boom-bust sequence** is Soros's regime taxonomy. Stages 1–4 are the boom (trend → reinforcement → testing → far-from-equilibrium); stage 5 is the "moment of truth" (gap between belief and reality is recognized); stages 6–8 are the bust (twilight → crossover → crash). The trade is positioning *across* the stage transitions, not classifying which box you're in. Source: [Acquirer's Multiple stage breakdown](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2022/08/george-soros-the-boom-bust-cycle-explained/); [Mises QJAE academic treatment](https://cdn.mises.org/qjae7_3_3.pdf).
- **Implication for our framework.** Reflexivity is hard to operationalize systematically (it's about *which signals matter when* — the BoE rate-defense in 1992 was a reflexivity signal because it was self-defeating; in another regime the same hike would be supportive). For an empirical / signal-driven system, the Soros contribution is **a flag for regime-transition probability** rather than a regime axis: when we see policy desperation (ZLB rate cuts at all-time-high asset prices, fiscal-monetary coordination at peak debt/GDP), that's a Soros-flag for elevated stage-5/6 transition risk.
