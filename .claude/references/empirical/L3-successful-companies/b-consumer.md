# L3-b — Consumer Multi-Bagger Case Studies

Scope: durable consumer/retail/brand multi-baggers across eras — KO (1900s industrial age compounder), MCD (post-war/Cold War expansion), NKE (60s-onward brand era), SBUX (90s-onward premium-experience), COST (warehouse club model since 1976), LULU (athleisure 2000s+), HD (big-box DIY), WMT (rural-retail 70s+), DIS (IP-flywheel — practitioner choice). The unifying lens: brand + distribution + capital allocation against the specific decade's consumer-spending regime.

## Section B — Distilled patterns (consumer-specific)

1. **Brand pricing power compounds asymmetrically.** Coca-Cola has held real pricing power for 130+ years; the New Coke fiasco of 1985 demonstrated brand resilience under self-inflicted damage. Buffett's 1988 KO thesis was explicitly about pricing power + global distribution, and his $1.3B initial investment now pays >$700M annually in dividends alone (>50% annual cash-on-cash). *Sources: Picture Perfect Portfolios KO, Berkshire 2024 letter.*

2. **Cost-leadership consumer models require religious cultural discipline to maintain across CEO transitions.** Costco caps margins at 14% (branded) / 15% (private label) by *policy*, refuses to advertise, and >70% of operating profit comes from membership fees. WMT's "every day low price" philosophy survived from Walton through McMillon. The cultural-discipline transmission mechanism is the differentiator — consumer cost leaders that loosened discipline (JCPenney, Sears) collapsed. *Sources: Quartr Costco, 25IQ Sinegal, Walmart strategy doc.*

3. **The hidden real-estate / financial-engineering layer is often the actual compounder, not the consumer-facing product.** McDonald's is famously a real estate company (37.7B real estate on balance sheet, $7.5B annual rental income from franchisees, 99% of total assets). The hamburger franchise generates traffic that justifies the rent. *Sources: Strategy Story MCD, Motley Fool MCD.*

4. **Founder-operator skin-in-the-game during the formative decade is near-universal.** Walton, Schultz, Kroc, Sinegal/Brotman, Marcus/Blank, Knight, Wilson — all founders or founder-equivalent operators who held large equity stakes through the multi-bag period. The transition to professional management often (not always) marked the deceleration phase. *Sources: Shoe Dog, Built from Scratch, Acquired Starbucks.*

5. **International expansion creates the second leg of multi-bag returns for consumer brands.** KO international was 80% of profit by 1996. SBUX China + Japan opened the second decade of returns. NKE international > NA revenue today. The pattern: dominate domestic → license/own international expansion → capture incremental profit pools. *Sources: Picture Perfect KO, Quartr Schultz, Tandfonline Knight.*

6. **The "cult brand" pattern (LULU, NKE, AAPL-as-consumer-brand) requires (a) identity/values association, (b) premium pricing, (c) narrative reinforcement.** Lululemon's "Manifesto," Nike's athlete endorsements, Apple's design ethos — all serve as identity affirmation rather than purely functional purchases. Premium prices then become a feature (status signal) rather than a friction. *Sources: HavStrategy LULU, Try Maverick LULU, Lightspeed Knight.*

7. **Membership/subscription economics inside a consumer business are the underrated compounding lever.** COST membership fees (>net income), AMZN Prime (cross-listed in tech sub-file but consumer pattern), gym memberships (LULU Mirror tried, failed), club retailing models (BJ's, Sam's). The variable: do members get differentiated value, or is it a discount-card scheme? *Sources: Quartr Costco, Cascade Costco.*

8. **Capital efficiency in consumer is via working-capital negative business models, not capex avoidance.** Costco turns inventory faster than it pays suppliers (negative cash conversion cycle). Same for WMT, MCD (franchisees fund the buildout). High inventory turns + supplier float is the consumer-retail equivalent of SaaS deferred revenue. *Sources: Cascade Costco, DigitalDefynd Walmart.*

9. **The right brand in the right decade.** KO was right for the urbanizing/refrigeration era (1900s-50s). MCD was right for the post-war suburban/automobile era (1950s-80s). NKE was right for the fitness/branded-consumer era (1980s-2000s). SBUX was right for the white-collar third-place era (1990s-2010s). LULU was right for the athleisure/wellness era (2010s-20s). Brands that overstayed their original decade (Sears, JCPenney) decayed; those that re-invented (NKE post-Air, MCD post-McCafe) extended. *Sources: cross-cutting; particularly Acquired SBUX, Tandfonline NKE.*

10. **Same-store sales > new-store growth as a quality signal in mature consumer.** Buffett's See's Candies was about pricing power without unit growth. Costco's same-club sales are the cleanest health metric. Companies that grew via store count but had declining SSS (Starbucks 2007-08, MCD various periods) showed the cracks before the broader market noticed. *Sources: Berkshire 2024 letter, 25IQ Sinegal.*

11. **Buyback discipline + dividend growth, not transformative M&A, characterizes mature consumer compounders.** WMT 53 consecutive dividend hikes. KO 60+ years dividend growth. MCD/HD/NKE all dividend aristocrats. Consumer M&A track record is poor (KO/Glaceau, KO/Costa, JNJ-tier consumer mergers): the brand acquisitions rarely produce the projected synergies. *Sources: Walmart 2025 IR, Berkshire KO position.*

12. **Founder departure is a critical inflection — sometimes positive (Apple/Cook capital return), sometimes negative (SBUX post-Schultz I, post-Schultz II).** The consumer pattern: founder builds; first professional CEO either consolidates discipline (Brotman→Sinegal continuity) or loses it (Schultz returns; Sears post-Sears family). *Sources: Acquired SBUX, Quartr Schultz.*

13. **Format innovation in retail is rare and underpriced.** Walton's discount + rural location combination. Sinegal's warehouse-club + membership combination. Marcus/Blank's big-box + knowledge-staff combination. Each was a *new format*, not a better execution of an existing one. The format innovation was the entry ticket to multi-bag returns. *Sources: Built from Scratch, Quartr Costco, Walton legacy.*

14. **DTC / e-commerce is a margin-rerating opportunity but rarely a multi-bag creator on its own** (the BYND/PTON cases in subfile d are warnings). Brands that successfully added DTC alongside wholesale (NKE) compounded; pure-DTC plays were more fragile. *Sources: Tandfonline NKE; cross-reference subfile d.*

15. **Real per-capita disposable-income growth is the sector's macro tailwind.** US real consumer spending compounded ~2-3% over 50 years; that's the floor of the addressable market growth. The multi-baggers took share during this — KO globally, COST in club retail, LULU in premium athleisure. The sector tailwind is necessary but not sufficient. *Sources: cross-cutting macro context.*

## Section C — Open questions / disagreements

- **Is Costco's model replicable internationally at the same margins?** International stores show strong unit economics in markets with similar consumer profiles (Canada, UK, Japan), weaker in others. Bear thesis: emerging markets won't pay membership fees at the rate that funds the model. Bull thesis: discretionary income growth will drive it.
- **Did Tim Cook outperform Steve Jobs on a per-dollar basis?** Bull camp says no — Jobs' product-led return per dollar invested was higher. Bear camp says yes — Cook's services + buyback strategy created compounding even after the iPhone supercycle peaked. (Cross-listed in subfile a but relevant to the consumer-brand framing here.)
- **How durable is the LULU cult brand if athleisure becomes commoditized?** Athletic-apparel comoditization risk (similar to GAP→old navy→specialty) is the bear case. NKE's longevity is the bull case. Unresolved.
- **Did Sears's decline have to happen?** Counterfactual debate (subfile d covers extensively): was Sears's mall-anchor format un-fixable, or was it the Lampert real-estate financialization that killed it?
- **Will Howard Schultz's "third place" thesis survive WFH/coffee-on-demand?** SBUX domestic same-store sales have been pressured 2023-25. Open question whether the next CEO can re-energize the in-store experience or whether the format peaked.
- **Are dividend aristocrats overrated?** Concentration in dividend-aristocrat indices has produced returns but with high tail-risk concentration in a few names (KO, JNJ, PG). Some practitioners argue these are "fake compounders" in a low-rate era.

## Notes on selection
- DIS included as practitioner choice — it tests the IP-flywheel pattern, distinct from durable-goods or experiential consumer.
- WMT included for both the rural-format-innovation pattern and as a cross-era control to LULU/SBUX (commodity vs premium consumer outcomes).
- The KO row deliberately covers the longest time horizon to anchor the "100+ year compounder" extreme of the distribution.
