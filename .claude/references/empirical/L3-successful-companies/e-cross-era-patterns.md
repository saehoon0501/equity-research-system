# L3-e — Cross-Era Patterns: What Reliably Distinguishes Multi-Baggers From Look-Alikes

This is the **load-bearing file** of the L3 lane. The question it answers: across tech, consumer, financials, healthcare, cyclicals, and across the PC / internet / mobile / cloud / AI eras — which patterns recur reliably, which are era-bound, and which are mostly noise?

The patterns below are tested against the cases in subfiles a, b, c (multi-bagger winners) and *especially* against the counterfactuals in subfile d (look-alikes that failed). Where a pattern is supported by winners but does not also distinguish them from failures, it is downgraded.

This file is for the "next Palantir" question. Use it as a checklist when a name-discovery skill surfaces a candidate.

## Section B — Cross-era patterns (the synthesis)

The patterns are categorized by confidence: **HIGH** = pattern holds across all 5+ eras and clearly distinguishes winners from look-alikes; **MEDIUM** = holds in most eras / requires nuance; **CONTESTED** = important hypothesis that is not yet empirically settled.

### HIGH-confidence patterns

1. **[CEO/founder duration ≥ 15 years correlates with multi-bag outcome.]** Verified in winners across all 5 eras: PC era (Gates), internet (Bezos, Page/Brin), mobile (Zuckerberg, Cook for value-extraction phase), cloud (Nadella from a cultural pivot), AI (Huang since 1993). Distinguishes winners from look-alikes in subfile d (Holmes/Theranos, Neumann/WeWork, Pearson/Valeant all had short tenure or rapid CEO turnover at the moment of stress). The mechanism is *consistent capital allocation through multiple cycles*. NB: Length alone is insufficient (Welch/GE, Chambers/Cisco are long-tenure winners-then-losers); duration must combine with reinvestment discipline. *Cases supporting: AMZN, MSFT, NVDA, GOOGL, META, BRK, COST (Sinegal), CSU (Leonard), DHR. Cases contradicting: GE/Welch (long tenure, decline post-departure), CSCO/Chambers, SHLD/Lampert. Confidence: HIGH.*

2. **[Per-share value as the explicit primary metric beats revenue/earnings totals.]** The Outsiders CEO archetype, formalized: management that prioritizes per-share metrics (FCF/share, book value/share, EPS) consistently outperforms management that prioritizes total revenue or absolute earnings. This is the through-line from Singleton (Teledyne 1960s-80s buyback of 90% of shares) → Buffett → Murphy → Stiritz → Leonard (CSU) → Cook (AAPL). The opposite pattern — total-revenue-maximization without per-share discipline — characterizes WeWork, late-era GE, Valeant. *Cases supporting: BRK, AAPL post-2013, AMZN-Jassy era, CSU, DHR. Cases contradicting: GE-Welch (ostensibly used per-share metrics but the underlying acquisition-treadmill financialized this), WeWork (total revenue / "Community-adjusted EBITDA" focus). Confidence: HIGH.*

3. **[Reinvestment at high incremental ROIC matters more than absolute ROIC.]** Mayer's "100-Baggers" finding, confirmed by Constellation Software's KPI ("ROIC + organic growth"), DBS, BRK. Companies that maintain >15% ROIIC over 15+ years deliver multi-bag returns; those with high ROIC but no reinvestable opportunity (mature consumer staples) deliver dividend-and-buyback compounds at lower rates. *Cases supporting: NVDA (CUDA reinvestment), AMZN (AWS reinvestment), COST (membership-flywheel reinvestment), CSU. Cases contradicting: KO has high ROIC but limited reinvestment opportunity, hence is a "good not great" compounder vs. AMZN-style. Confidence: HIGH.*

4. **[Original product is rarely the multi-bagger creator; the second/third pivot is.]** AMZN: books → AWS. NVDA: gaming GPU → AI infra. MSFT: OS → Azure. AAPL: Mac → iPhone. META: desktop social → mobile/Reels. ADBE: license → Cloud. UNH: insurance → Optum. The original idea gets the company to escape velocity; the pivot creates the multi-bag. Failure-mode mirror: companies that *cannot* pivot (Kodak, Polaroid, Blockbuster, Nokia, BB) collapse despite being category leaders. *Cases supporting: nearly all subfile-a/b/c winners. Cases contradicting: KO (durable single-product compound, but at a slower rate). Confidence: HIGH.*

5. **[Founder/CEO equity stake of >5% sustained through multi-bag period correlates with discipline.]** Skin-in-the-game thesis. Buffett held BRK, Bezos AMZN, Zuckerberg META, Page/Brin GOOGL, Sinegal COST, Walton WMT, Knight NKE — all retained substantial equity through the multi-bag. Counterfactual: Holmes/Theranos held founder equity but the underlying business was fraud (so equity doesn't *guarantee* discipline; but absence of equity is a stronger negative signal than presence is positive). *Cases supporting: most subfile-a winners. Cases contradicting: GE post-Welch professional CEOs; some declines despite founder equity (e.g., LULU's Wilson holdings during 2013 ouster). Confidence: HIGH for negative signal (lack of skin-in-game = warning); MEDIUM for positive.*

6. **[Switching costs (training, data, ecosystem) > pricing power (brand) for tech moats; reverse for consumer.]** ISRG (surgeon training), MSFT (Office files), GOOGL (search-history personalization), CUDA (developer ecosystem), V/MA (merchant integration) — switching cost is the durable moat. KO, NKE, LULU rely more on brand-pricing-power. The hybrid (AAPL ecosystem + brand) is the strongest durability profile. *Cases supporting: cross-cutting. Cases contradicting: SBUX brand+experience but lower switching cost = more vulnerable to format changes. Confidence: HIGH within categories.*

7. **[Capital efficiency (FCF / invested capital) is bimodal in tech: software platforms ≥40% mature; hardware ≤20%.]** This is why MSFT/GOOGL/META/V/MA/ADBE compound faster than even successful hardware (CSCO, even NVDA at maturity). NVDA's current run is a hardware co. selling near-monopoly software (CUDA) at premium margin; if CUDA's lock-in degrades, NVDA's economics revert toward hardware. *Cases supporting: subfile a/c. Cases contradicting: NVDA itself is the in-progress test. Confidence: HIGH.*

8. **[Fraud / governance failure has a consistent pattern signature.]** Across Theranos, Enron, Valeant, WeWork: (a) charismatic CEO with mystique; (b) board lacking domain expertise or co-opted; (c) accounting innovations or novel metrics; (d) secrecy under "trade secret" or "competitive" cover; (e) whistleblower or short-seller research dismissed without rebuttal; (f) related-party transactions or insider-aligned counterparties. Any 3+ of these = high alert. *Cases: subfile d. Confidence: HIGH (pattern is reproducible).*

### MEDIUM-confidence patterns

9. **[Customer-concentration trajectory matters more than absolute concentration.]** A growing company with 30% top-customer concentration that's *decreasing* is healthier than one with 10% concentration that's *increasing*. PLTR's concentration trajectory (government → diversified commercial) is the live test case. *Cases supporting: PLTR direction (decreasing); ISRG (decreasing per-hospital reliance as installed base grows). Cases contradicting: companies whose concentration grew with single-customer dependence (CRSP early-era, some defense contractors). Confidence: MEDIUM.*

10. **[TAM expansion via adjacencies is the most reliable growth path; new-market entry is risky.]** AMZN: books → CD → all retail → Prime → AWS → ads. AAPL: Mac → iPod → iPhone → Services. UNH: insurance → PBM → care delivery → analytics. The mechanism: existing customer relationships amortize CAC across new revenue streams. New-market-entry (Google Cloud vs. AWS, Apple Car never shipped) has higher failure rate. *Cases supporting: AMZN, AAPL, MSFT, UNH, DHR. Cases contradicting: Tesla's Model 3-and-beyond is technically adjacency but executed in capital-intensive manner. Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH.*

11. **[Network effects compound only when accompanied by switching costs.]** META has both (network + history). GOOGL Search has both (network from indexing + personalization-data switching cost). Pure two-sided marketplaces without lock-in (Groupon, early Yelp, GrubHub) tend to compete on take-rate and don't compound at multi-bag rates. V/MA have network effects supported by merchant-integration switching costs. *Cases supporting: META, GOOGL, V/MA. Cases contradicting: Groupon, Yelp, GrubHub. Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH.*

12. **[Optionality value (call options on adjacent markets) is real but hard to underwrite.]** AWS in 2003 was an internal-infrastructure project; no analyst priced it. CUDA in 2007 was a money-loser for ~10 years. Reality Labs / Apple Vision Pro / Optimus Robotics are current candidates of unknown outcome. The pattern: optionality bets that *eventually* paid off were sustained for ~5-10 years before market validation. *Cases supporting: AMZN AWS, NVDA CUDA, META mobile pivot. Cases contradicting: many failed optionality bets (Google+, Microsoft mobile, Amazon Fire Phone). Confidence: MEDIUM (asymmetric — winners are huge, but base rate is poor).*

13. **[Founder + dual-class share structure is a positive signal in tech, neutral in consumer/financials.]** GOOGL, META, PLTR, FB-pre-2012, NVDA founder authority — all benefited from founder voting control. Some consumer dual-class structures (NYT, Comcast, Ford) are correlated with mediocre returns. The mechanism: in tech, where pivots are necessary, founder control enables long-term capex against street pressure; in consumer, where steady execution matters more, control is less differential. *Cases supporting: subfile-a tech. Cases contradicting: NYT Class B, Comcast, Ford Class B — non-tech dual class. Confidence: MEDIUM.*

14. **[Buyback discipline vs. price matters more than buyback volume.]** Outsiders-CEO playbook: Singleton bought back 90% of Teledyne shares but did so opportunistically at low valuations. AAPL's $841B buyback is mostly executed at increasing share prices (less optimal but still effective due to share count reduction). SHLD/Lampert bought back $5B while gutting capex — destructive use of buybacks. *Cases supporting: BRK, BRK-style allocators. Cases contradicting: SHLD/Lampert, GE-Immelt buybacks. Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH.*

15. **[Valuation discipline matters even for great businesses.]** Cisco at 2000 (220x P/E, 70% market share) rewarded the business but punished investors for 25 years. Same business success / different stock outcome at different entry prices. The Harding Loevner NVIDIA-vs-CSCO comparison is the live test. *Cases supporting: CSCO post-2000, GE post-2000. Cases contradicting: AMZN compounded despite expensive valuations because reinvestment kept pulling forward. Confidence: MEDIUM (the "great business at any price" claim is partially false).*

### CONTESTED patterns

16. **[Narrative reflexivity (the Soros / Drukenmiller idea — narrative drives fundamentals which validate narrative)] is real but operates differently in different eras.** TSLA bull thesis is largely reflexive. PLTR's 2024-25 surge has reflexive elements. Bezos at AMZN benefited from reflexivity in 1999, then was punished for it 2000-02, then re-rose on actual execution 2003+. The contested question: *which reflexive narratives convert to fundamental compounding vs. which collapse?* Empirically unresolved. *Cases supporting: TSLA, PLTR (live). Cases contradicting: AMZN survived its reflexive collapse via actual unit economics. Confidence: CONTESTED.*

17. **[Capital allocation in cyclical industries is compounding-decisive but rarely understood by the market.]** XOM 43-year dividend streak and capital discipline (vs. CHK and other shale leverers) is one of the cleanest cyclicals examples. CAT services revenue smoothing. The pattern is real but harder to identify in real-time because cycles obscure underlying discipline. *Cases supporting: XOM, CVX, CAT, DE. Cases contradicting: many cyclical names that look disciplined at peak then over-lever (energy majors 2014, REITs 2007). Confidence: MEDIUM but harder to time.*

18. **[Owner-manager culture transmission across CEO transitions is critical and hard to assess externally.]** COST Sinegal → Jelinek transition preserved discipline. AAPL Jobs → Cook preserved (and arguably improved on margin discipline). MSFT Ballmer → Nadella was a positive cultural pivot. GE Welch → Immelt was a destructive transition. The empirical question: *can outsiders assess cultural transmission before it shows up in financials?* Open. *Confidence: CONTESTED but high-stakes.*

19. **[Founder mystique without underlying execution = fraud risk.]** Holmes/Theranos, Neumann/WeWork, Lay-Skilling/Enron all had cultivated mystique. Buffett/Bezos/Huang/Cook had mystique *plus* execution track records. The inference: *charismatic founder is necessary but not sufficient; execution validation must accompany.* The challenge: in early-stage companies, execution validation is ambiguous, which is when the fraud lookalikes succeed at fundraising. *Confidence: HIGH for the negative signal (mystique without execution), but timing of the signal is hard.*

20. **[The "right thing in the right decade" thesis has substantial era-dependency.]** PC era selected for OS/PC ecosystems; internet era for logistics + ad networks; mobile era for app stores + photo-sharing; cloud era for SaaS; AI era for compute infrastructure + foundation models. A great founder + great team + great capital allocation in the *wrong* decade for their thesis fails (Pets.com). The implication for forward identification: *match candidate businesses to the macro/infra-stack era they're operating in.* Pets.com idea ≠ Chewy outcome despite same model — because broadband + recurring billing + last-mile logistics matured between. *Confidence: HIGH for the principle; MEDIUM-HIGH for forward application.*

## Section C — Open questions / disagreements

- **Is the Bezos-style "long-term reinvestment, never profits" model dependent on 0% interest rates?** Active debate. Bull: AMZN's playbook is replicable in any rate regime where ROIIC > cost of capital. Bear: 2022 tech reset showed how much the model relied on cheap capital.
- **Is NVDA at 2024-26 the next CSCO 2000?** Harding Loevner's analogy is structurally compelling (concentration of capex spend, peak market position, expanding multiple) but NVDA bulls argue the recurring-software economics are different. Resolution likely 2027-28.
- **Are dual-class structures fair to outside shareholders, regardless of return outcome?** Governance debate. Many index providers exclude or reduce weights for dual-class. From a return-prediction lens, dual-class in tech has correlated with multi-bag, but ESG/governance considerations are separate.
- **Did the multi-bag winners' success have substantial luck content?** Selection bias warning. We are studying winners; the corresponding losers (subfile d) are smaller in number but exist. Survivorship-bias-aware practitioners (Mauboussin, Mlodinow) argue luck-skill weighting is significant.
- **Can private equity / activist investors create multi-bag returns from non-multi-bag businesses?** Bermuda-style 3G, Berkshire-style, KKR-style. Empirically yes for some; but the public-market multi-bag thesis usually requires the operating business to compound, not financial engineering.
- **Is the "secular vs. cyclical" categorization reliable, or do today's seculars become tomorrow's cyclicals?** GOOGL and META were once-secular advertising stories; advertising has cyclical components revealed in 2022. NVDA's "secular AI" story has cyclical capex-cycle components. Treating any business as fully secular is risky over 10+ year horizons.

## Application: the candidate-evaluation checklist

When a name-discovery skill produces a candidate, run it against the patterns above:

**Tier-A signals (strong positive)**
- Founder-CEO with 10+ years tenure AND 5%+ equity stake AND meaningful past pivot
- ROIIC sustained >15% with reinvestment runway
- Customer concentration trajectory decreasing
- Per-share value metrics in management's published priorities
- Switching-cost moat present (not just brand or first-mover)

**Tier-B signals (positive context)**
- Adjacency-expansion track record
- Capital-light / FCF-generative core business
- Macro/infra-stack era aligned with thesis
- Buyback or capital-return discipline at sensible valuations

**Red flags (any 1 = caution; any 3+ = high alert / exit)**
- Charismatic CEO + governance failures (board lacks domain expertise)
- Novel accounting metrics ("Community-adjusted EBITDA" type)
- Whistleblower or short-seller research dismissed without rebuttal
- Related-party transactions or insider-aligned counterparties
- Total-revenue maximization over per-share value
- Pandemic-era or other regime-trade tailwind treated as permanent
- Roll-up growth obscuring zero organic growth
- Real-estate or balance-sheet financialization in operating business
- Peak market share + extreme valuation (Cisco-2000 / GE-2000 setup)

**Era-fit check**
- Is the macro/tech/regulatory environment for this thesis at year ~2-3 of a multi-decade cycle, or year ~10? Pets.com vs. Chewy is a cleaner test for this — same idea, vastly different decade.

The candidate must clear Tier-A or strong Tier-B + zero strong red flags + favorable era-fit to qualify as a serious multi-bagger candidate.

This checklist is not infallible — survivorship bias means we are pattern-matching primarily on winners. But it shifts the base rate by deliberately incorporating the failure-mode catalog from sub-file d into the screening.

## Section B.2 — New cross-era patterns

### HIGH-confidence patterns (additions)

21. **[The fraud signature pattern (existing #8) is invariant across sectors and decades — confirmed strongly by FTX.]** Re-test of Section B Pattern #8 against the 2022 FTX collapse: every component of the original 6-element signature (mystique + governance void + accounting innovations + secrecy + dismissed bears + related-party transactions) reproduced with crypto-era specifics. The 2001 Enron / 2018 Theranos / 2016 Valeant / 2019 WeWork → 2022 FTX progression confirms the *signature is sector-invariant*. The Section D pattern table row for fraud signature is upgraded from "all 4 cases support" to "5 cases support across 4 sectors and 25-year span." *Confidence: HIGH (further reinforced).*

22. **[SPAC-mechanism structural defect: a sector-wide failure pattern, not just individual fraud.]** The 2020-2022 SPAC vintage's average -54% to -67% post-merger return (academic finding from Bruck SSRN, Yale JReg) is not just a collection of fraud cases (Nikola, Lordstown, Hyzon, Clover) — it is a *structural* finding that the SPAC sponsor-promote mechanism creates over-projection incentives. The L3-screening implication: any de-SPAC name carries a structural liability discount that should not be ignored, even absent fraud-specific red flags. This is a new pattern not present in the original 20 (the original lane focused on individual-case fraud signatures, not mechanism-level structural failures). *Confidence: HIGH.*

23. **[Meme-stock distortion is a regime signal, not a fundamental signal.]** Adds GME, AMC, BBBY, Hertz to the empirical record. The pattern: socially-coordinated retail flow can decouple price from fundamentals for 6-24 months, after which fundamentals reassert (BBBY → bankruptcy 2023, Hertz → equity wiped in restructuring, GME → continued retail decline). The L3-implication: a name in active meme-distortion phase is *not* evaluable on subfile-a/b/c criteria during the distortion; the prudent approach is to wait for re-anchoring or avoid entirely. This connects to but is distinct from the original Pattern #16 (narrative reflexivity) — meme distortion is a stronger, more transient form. *Confidence: HIGH.*

24. **[Crypto-era boom-bust (Coinbase as paradigm) is a regime/cycle trade, NOT a "right thing in right decade" play.]** Original Pattern #20 (right thing in right decade) frames era-fit as a positive multi-bag mechanism. The 2020-2022 crypto-era test confirms an important refinement: the *underlying asset class* (crypto) had era-fit, but the *publicly-traded equity vehicles* (COIN, MSTR-as-Bitcoin-proxy, MARA, RIOT) operated as leveraged-cycle ETFs rather than secular compounders. Pattern #20 is therefore qualified: era-fit applies to companies that *structurally capture* the secular shift (CUDA, AWS, mobile-OS) — not to companies that merely *trade exposure to* it. Trading-volume-dependent business models in cyclical asset classes inherit the cycle, not the secular trend. *Confidence: HIGH (refines existing Pattern #20).*

25. **[Same-era same-sector divergence: thesis correctness is necessary but not sufficient.]** Lyft vs. Uber (-82% vs +96% from IPO close as of 2025), Box vs. MongoDB (+37% vs multi-bag), Cloudera (taken private) vs. Snowflake (publicly-traded category leader despite reset), Allbirds (-99%) vs. Warby Parker (recovering compounder). Each pair: same era, same broad sector thesis, vastly different outcomes. The mechanism: *winner-takes-most economics within the right era still requires sub-pattern selection* — broader optionality, faster path to scale economics, durable switching costs, founder-aligned capital allocation. The L3-screening implication: when two same-era same-sector candidates exist, the presence of a thesis-validated peer is the *minimum* not the *sufficient* condition. *Confidence: HIGH.*

### MEDIUM/CONTESTED additions

26. **[The "post-crisis turnaround compounder" archetype (CMG-Niccol, DPZ-Doyle).]** A specific sub-pattern: when a consumer brand suffers a discrete brand-damaging event (E. coli, "our pizza is bad" customer perception), an outsider-CEO with operating credibility can produce 10-100x returns by (a) public-acknowledgement reset, (b) operational fix, (c) tech-enabled distribution upgrade, (d) menu/product innovation. Cross-cases: Niccol's CMG (~800% return), Doyle's DPZ (~100,000% total return since 2008 trough). Test cases pending: Niccol's Starbucks tenure (2024-), other recent appointments. The pattern is real but base-rate-uncertain (most brand-damaged consumer companies don't recover). *Confidence: MEDIUM (asymmetric outcomes; identifying the right CEO is hard ex ante).*

27. **[The "managed scarcity" luxury moat (Hermès) is a distinct compounder pattern from brand-pricing-power (KO, NKE).]** Hermès caps Birkin/Kelly production at 70K/year, lets secondary-market prices float to 2-3x retail, and explicitly refuses growth that would dilute scarcity. This is a stronger and more durable moat than conventional brand-pricing-power because the product itself is supply-constrained at the manufacturer level. Cross-reference subfile b's distinction between cult-brand premium (LULU, NKE, AAPL-as-consumer) and managed-scarcity luxury (Hermès, Ferrari, very high-end watchmakers). The pattern is rare but very durable when present. *Confidence: HIGH for the pattern; HIGH for Hermès specifically; MEDIUM for the broader luxury sector (LVMH's volume model is the more common variant).*

28. **[The capital-equipment 20-year compounding moat (ASML).]** Different from software/network moats (subfile a Pattern #5 / e Pattern #6) and different from brand moats (subfile b). ASML's monopoly arises from cumulative R&D + supplier-codevelopment + customer-codevelopment over 20+ years that no competitor can compress. Test of replicability: KLA, AMAT (other semicap leaders) have similar but less-extreme positions; the EUV-monopoly is the extreme case. The L3-implication: in deep-tech capital equipment, time itself can be the moat — a pattern absent from the original 20. *Confidence: HIGH (specific to ASML/EUV); MEDIUM for broader replicability.*

## Section D.3 — Pattern interactions surfaced by the deepening

The deepening pass surfaced two pattern *interactions* that are themselves predictive:

**Interaction A: Fraud signature × SPAC mechanism = compound liability.** When the SPAC structural defect (Pattern 22 above) intersects with the canonical fraud signature (Pattern 8), the result is the Nikola/Clover/Hyzon cluster — all SPACs that were also frauds. The interaction is multiplicative, not additive: SPAC structural defects mean fraud-pattern checking should be *more* (not less) rigorous for de-SPAC names, because the disclosure regime is asymmetrically lax compared to traditional IPOs.

**Interaction B: Meme distortion × bankruptcy proximity = absolute-priority-rule violation.** When a meme-distorted name is also approaching bankruptcy (Hertz 2020, BBBY 2022-23), retail buyers at distortion-elevated prices have negative expected return because the absolute priority rule will eventually wipe their equity. The combination is not just risky — it is *mathematically* losing absent unlikely outcomes. UChicago Law Review's "Hertz Maneuver" academic paper formalizes this.

These interactions add a layer to the candidate-evaluation checklist: compound-pattern interactions can be more diagnostic than individual-pattern checks.
